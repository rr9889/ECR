Canon Books

Introduction

The period leading up to the Council of Nicaea in 325 AD was critical in shaping the New Testament canon. The writings of Church Fathers such as Irenaeus, Tertullian, Origen, and Eusebius, along with documents like the Muratorian Fragment, provide insight into which books were widely accepted among early Christians. This article highlights the key texts recognized during this era.

Matthew

 

The Church Fathers universally recognized Matthew as the author of the first Gospel, written in Hebrew (or Aramaic) for a Jewish audience.

Irenaeus of Lyons stated that "Matthew also issued a written Gospel among the Hebrews in their own dialect" (Against Heresies, 3.1.1), emphasizing its Jewish focus. Papias of Hierapolis noted that "Matthew compiled the sayings [of Jesus] in the Hebrew language" (Expositions of the Sayings of the Lord), highlighting its role in preserving Jesus’ teachings and fulfilling Old Testament prophecies. Origen affirmed Matthew’s Gospel as the first written and widely used (Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History, 6.25.4), and Eusebius of Caesarea confirmed its acceptance as Scripture (Ecclesiastical History, 3.24.6). Matthew’s Gospel was valued for its apostolic origin, Jewish perspective, and emphasis on Jesus as the Messiah.

Mark

 

The Church Fathers unanimously affirmed that Mark wrote his Gospel based on the teachings of Peter, making it an accurate and reliable account of Christ’s life and ministry.

Papias of Hierapolis (early 2nd century) stated that Mark, as Peter’s interpreter, "wrote down accurately, though not in order, whatsoever he remembered of the things said or done by Christ" (Expositions of the Sayings of the Lord). This connection to Peter gave Mark’s Gospel apostolic authority, even though it was not written by an apostle directly. Irenaeus of Lyons (late 2nd century) also confirmed Mark’s role as Peter’s disciple, emphasizing the Gospel’s reliability (Against Heresies, 3.1.1). The early Church accepted Mark’s Gospel as Scripture, valuing its close association with Peter’s eyewitness testimony.

Luke

The Church Fathers recognized Luke as a companion of Paul and the author of both the Gospel of Luke and the Acts of the Apostles.

Irenaeus of Lyons stated that "Luke, the companion of Paul, recorded in a book the Gospel preached by him" (Against Heresies, 3.1.1), highlighting Luke’s role in preserving Paul’s teachings. Tertullian (early 3rd century) also affirmed Luke’s Gospel as authoritative, noting its connection to Paul (Against Marcion, 4.2). The early Church valued Luke’s Gospel for its detailed and orderly account of Christ’s life, as well as its emphasis on the universality of the gospel message. Luke’s status as a close associate of Paul ensured its acceptance as Scripture.

John

The Church Fathers universally acknowledged John, the beloved disciple, as the author of the Gospel of John.

Irenaeus of Lyons wrote that "John, the disciple of the Lord, did himself publish a Gospel during his residence at Ephesus" (Against Heresies, 3.1.1), emphasizing its apostolic origin. Clement of Alexandria (late 2nd century) described John’s Gospel as a "spiritual Gospel," written to complement the more historical accounts of the Synoptic Gospels (Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History, 6.14.7). The early Church revered John’s Gospel for its profound theological insights, particularly its emphasis on Christ’s divinity and the incarnation. Its apostolic authorship and unique perspective made it an indispensable part of the New Testament canon.

Acts of the Apostles

The Church Fathers universally recognized the Acts of the Apostles as Scripture and attributed its authorship to Luke, the companion of Paul.

Irenaeus of Lyons stated that "Luke also, the companion of Paul, recorded in a book the Gospel preached by him, and the acts of the apostles" (Against Heresies, 3.1.1). This affirmed Luke’s authorship and the book’s connection to Paul’s ministry.Clement of Alexandria (late 2nd century) referred to Acts as authoritative, using it to support his teachings (Stromata, 5.12). Tertullian also accepted Acts as canonical, calling it "the Acts of the Apostles" and using it to defend orthodox Christianity (On Prescription Against Heretics, 22). Eusebius of Caesarea included Acts among the universally accepted books of the New Testament, noting its widespread use in the early Church (Ecclesiastical History, 3.25.1). The early Church valued Acts for its historical account of the early Church’s growth, the spread of the gospel, and the work of the Holy Spirit through the apostles, particularly Peter and Paul.

Pauline Epistles

The Pauline Epistles were universally accepted as authoritative Scripture by early Christian writers.

Clement of Rome frequently quoted Paul’s letters, especially 1 Corinthians and Romans, urging the Corinthian church to "take up the epistle of the blessed Paul the Apostle" (1 Clement, 47.1–3). Ignatius of Antioch referred to Paul as a model of faith and often alluded to his epistles, particularly Ephesians (Letter to the Ephesians, 12.2). Polycarp of Smyrna encouraged the Philippians to study Paul’s letters, stating they would "be built up in the faith" (Letter to the Philippians, 3.2).

By the late 2nd century, Irenaeus of Lyons extensively quoted Paul’s epistles, calling them "Scripture" and using them to defend orthodox Christianity (Against Heresies, 3.14.1). Tertullian defended Paul’s letters against Marcion, who had tried to edit them, arguing that the Ephesians, Philippians, and others were authoritative (Against Marcion, 5.17–21). Origen accepted all 13 Pauline epistles as authoritative and frequently quoted them in his commentaries. Eusebius of Caesarea listed all 13 as universally accepted, noting their widespread use (Ecclesiastical History, 3.3.5).

General Epistles

The Church Fathers universally recognized 1 Peter as an authoritative epistle written by the apostle Peter.

Irenaeus of Lyons (late 2nd century) quoted from 1 Peter, referring to it as Scripture and using it to defend orthodox teachings (Against Heresies, 4.9.2). Clement of Alexandria (late 2nd century) also cited 1 Peter, emphasizing its exhortations to holiness and its apostolic authority (Stromata, 4.20). Tertullian (early 3rd century) affirmed its widespread acceptance, stating that "Peter’s Epistle is recognized by all" (On Modesty, 20). Eusebius of Caesarea (early 4th century) included 1 Peter among the universally accepted books of the New Testament (Ecclesiastical History, 3.3.1). The early Church valued 1 Peter for its encouragement to persecuted Christians and its emphasis on living a holy life.

The Church Fathers also universally accepted 1 John as Scripture, attributing it to the apostle John.

Irenaeus of Lyons frequently quoted 1 John, calling it "the Epistle of John" and using it to combat heresies like Gnosticism (Against Heresies, 3.16.5). Clement of Alexandria referred to 1 John as authoritative, highlighting its teachings on love and truth (Stromata, 2.15). Origen (early 3rd century) affirmed 1 John as part of the undisputed books of the New Testament (Commentary on John). Eusebius of Caesarea confirmed its universal acceptance, noting its widespread use in the early Church (Ecclesiastical History, 3.25.2). The early Church valued 1 John for its profound theological insights, particularly its emphasis on love, truth, and the incarnation of Christ.

Revelation

The Book of Revelation (also called the Apocalypse of John) was widely accepted by many early Church Fathers as Scripture, though its canonicity was debated by some.

Justin Martyr (mid-2nd century) accepted Revelation as Scripture, referring to it as the work of "John, one of the apostles of Christ" (Dialogue with Trypho, 81). Irenaeus of Lyons (late 2nd century) strongly affirmed Revelation’s apostolic authorship, stating that it was written by "John, the disciple of the Lord" (Against Heresies, 4.20.11). He used it extensively to defend orthodox teachings, particularly on eschatology (the study of end times). Clement of Alexandria (late 2nd century) also accepted Revelation as authoritative, quoting from it in his writings (Stromata, 6.13). Tertullian (early 3rd century) regarded Revelation as Scripture, using it to support his arguments on the resurrection and the final judgment (On the Resurrection of the Flesh, 24).

Rejection Opinion - Dionysius of Alexandria argued Revelation was not written by John the Apostle due to differences in style, vocabulary, and theology compared to the Gospel and epistles of John. He suggested it might have been written by John the Elder, a different figure. Additionally, he urged careful handling of its symbolic content to avoid misuse.

Eusebius of Caesarea listed Revelation among the "disputed books" (antilegomena), noting some churches questioned its apostolic authorship. He acknowledged its wider acceptance in the Western Church but skepticism in the Eastern Church, partly due to associations with Montanism. Overall he acknowledged its use by early writers but documented ongoing debates about its authority.

Disputed Books

James

Origen said "The Epistle of James is said to be his." (Commentary on John). Origen acknowledged James as Scripture but noted its disputed status, as some questioned its apostolic authority.  Eusebius of Caesarea listed James among the "disputed books" (antilegomena), noting that while it was widely read, some churches questioned its canonicity (Ecclesiastical History, 3.25.3).  Jerome accepted James as canonical, attributing it to James, the brother of Jesus (Lives of Illustrious Men, 2).

2 Peter

Eusebius of Caesarea said "Peter’s second Epistle is disputed." (Ecclesiastical History, 3.3.1). Eusebius noted that 2 Peter was not as widely accepted as 1 Peter, with some early Christians questioning its authenticity due to differences in style and content. Origen stated "Peter left one Epistle... perhaps also a second, but this is doubted." (Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History, 6.25.8). Origen acknowledged its use but expressed uncertainty about its authorship. Jerome accepted 2 Peter as canonical, though he noted its disputed status (Lives of Illustrious Men, 1).

2 John

Origen stated "John wrote two Epistles... but the second is disputed." (Commentary on John). Origen recognized 2 John as part of the Johannine writings but noted that its brevity and limited circulation led some to question its authority.Eusebius of Caesarea listed 2 John among the "disputed books" (antilegomena), noting that while it was accepted by many, others doubted its canonicity (Ecclesiastical History, 3.25.3). Jerome accepted 2 John as canonical, though he acknowledged its disputed status (Lives of Illustrious Men, 9).

Epistle of Barnabas

Accepted Opinion - Clement of Alexandria highly valued the Epistle of Barnabas, quoting it extensively in his writings (Stromata, 2.6, 2.7, 2.20). He treated it as Scripture and used it to support his theological arguments. He referred to it as a "Catholic Epistle," indicating its widespread use in the early Church. Origen also held the Epistle of Barnabas in high regard, referring to it as a "useful" and "instructive" text (Commentary on Romans). However, he did not explicitly include it in his list of canonical books.

Rejected Opinion - Eusebius of Caesarea listed the Epistle of Barnabas among the "disputed books", noting that while it was widely read, it was not universally accepted as Scripture (Ecclesiastical History, 3.25.4). He classified it as a "spurious" work, meaning it was not considered part of the New Testament canon. Jerome acknowledged the Epistle of Barnabas as a valuable early Christian text but did not include it in his list of canonical books (Lives of Illustrious Men, 6).

Shepherd of Hermas

The Shepherd of Hermas was a popular and influential text in the early Church, but its canonicity was debated. Early Church Fathers like Irenaeus of Lyons, Clement of Alexandria, and Origen valued it for its teachings on repentance and moral living, often quoting it as Scripture or an authoritative text. However, others like Eusebius of Caesarea, Athanasius of Alexandria, and Jerome classified it as non-canonical, though they acknowledged its spiritual and instructional value. While widely read and respected, the Shepherd of Hermas was ultimately excluded from the New Testament canon due to doubts about its apostolic authorship and theological content. Despite this, it remains an important early Christian text, offering insights into the early Church’s emphasis on repentance, ethical living, and visionary teachings.

Apocryphal Gospels

  • Gospel of Thomas: Used by Gnostic Christians but rejected by the orthodox Church for its unorthodox teachings.

  • Gospel of Peter: Criticized for its docetic tendencies (denying Jesus’ humanity) and excluded from the canon.

  • Infancy Gospel of Thomas: Considered fanciful and unreliable, not accepted as Scripture.

  • Protoevangelium of James: Popular for its focus on Mary’s life, but not included in the canon.

Apocryphal Acts

  • Acts of Paul: Included the story of Thecla but rejected for its unorthodox teachings.

  • Acts of Peter: Focused on Peter’s miracles but excluded due to its association with docetism.

  • Acts of John: Contained Gnostic ideas and was rejected by the orthodox Church.

  • Acts of Andrew: Described Andrew’s missionary journeys, but its canonicity was disputed.

Apocryphal Epistles

  • Epistle to the Laodiceans: Attributed to Paul but considered spurious by the Church Fathers.

  • 3 Corinthians: Attributed to Paul but rejected for its lack of authenticity.

  • Epistle of Polycarp to the Philippians: Respected but not considered Scripture.

Apocalyptic and Visionary Literature

  • Apocalypse of Peter: Respected by some for its visions of heaven and hell, but excluded from the canon.

  • Apocalypse of Paul: Popular for its detailed account of Paul’s vision of the afterlife, but not accepted as Scripture.
