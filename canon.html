Canon Books

Accepted Consensus Books of the New Testament (95-325 A.D)
Category Books Affirmed by Church Fathers
Gospels Matthew, Mark, Luke, John Irenaeus, Against Heresies (3.1.1): Affirmed all four Gospels. Clement of Alexandria, Stromata (6.14.7). Origen, Commentary on Matthew; Ecclesiastical History (6.25.4). Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History (3.24.6). Polycarp, quoted from Matthew (Ehrman, 2003, p. 326). Justin Martyr: Affirmed Matthew, Luke, maybe John. Didache heavily quotes from Matthew and Luke (Van de Sandt, 2005).
Acts of the Apostles Acts of the Apostles Irenaeus: Against Heresies (3.1.1), Clement of Alexandria: Stromata (5.12), Tertullian: On Prescription Against Heretics (22), Eusebius: Ecclesiastical History (3.25.1). Origen (Homily 7.1 on Joshua).
Pauline Epistles Romans, 1 Corinthians, 2 Corinthians, Galatians, Ephesians, Philippians, Colossians, 1 Thessalonians, 2 Thessalonians, 1 Timothy, 2 Timothy, Titus, Philemon Clement of Rome: 1 Clement (47.1–3). Ignatius of Antioch: Letter to the Ephesians (12.2). Polycarp of Smyrna: Letter to the Philippians (3.2). Irenaeus: Against Heresies (3.14.1). Tertullian: Against Marcion (5.17–21). Origen: Commentaries. Eusebius: Ecclesiastical History (3.3.5).
General Epistles 1 Peter, 1 John Irenaeus: Against Heresies (4.9.2), Clement of Alexandria: Stromata (4.20), Tertullian: On Modesty (20), Origen: Commentary on John, Eusebius: Ecclesiastical History (3.3.1).
Revelation Revelation Irenaeus: Against Heresies (3.11.1; 4.20.11; 5.35.2) Clement of Alexandria: Who Is the Rich Man Who Shall Be Saved? (42); Stromata (6.13) Justin Martyr: Dialogue with Trypho (81) Tertullian: On the Resurrection of the Flesh (24); Against Marcion (3.14.3) Eusebius: Ecclesiastical History (3.25.3) Hippolytus: On Antichrist (36) Origen: Commentary on John (5.3).  Didache's description of the "path of death" bears similarities to Revelation (O'Loughlin, 2011).
Disputed Books (180 - 393 A.D.)
Category Books Notes
Disputed Epistles James, 2 Peter, 2 John Origen: Commentary on John and Ecclesiastical History (6.25.8) expressed doubts about James and 2 Peter. Eusebius: Ecclesiastical History (3.25.3) lists James and 2 Peter as disputed. Jerome: Lives of Illustrious Men (2) accepted James, though noting its disputed status.
Disputed Writings Epistle of Barnabas, Shepherd of Hermas Clement of Alexandria: Stromata (2.6, 2.7, 2.20) highly valued the Epistle of Barnabas, while Eusebius: Ecclesiastical History (3.25.4) listed it as disputed. Origen: Commentary on Romans appreciated Shepherd of Hermas but didn’t include it in his canon. Jerome: Lives of Illustrious Men (6) did not accept it as canonical.
Apocryphal Books Gospel of Thomas, Gospel of Peter, Infancy Gospel of Thomas, Protoevangelium of James, Acts of Paul, Acts of Peter, Acts of John, Acts of Andrew, Apocalypse of Peter, Apocalypse of Paul, Epistle to the Laodiceans, 3 Corinthians, Epistle of Polycarp to the Philippians, Diatessaron, Odes of Solomon, 1 Clement, 1 Enoch, Jubilees, Sinodos Irenaeus: Against Heresies (1.20.1), Tertullian: Against Marcion (5.17-21), Eusebius: Ecclesiastical History (3.25), rejected these apocryphal writings. Clement of Alexandria: Stromata (6.13) acknowledged some of these works but did not treat them as canonical. Jerome: Lives of Illustrious Men (6) rejected most apocryphal books.
Additional Books (By Church Region)
Region Additional Books Notes
Syrian Church Diatessaron, Odes of Solomon Eusebius: Ecclesiastical History (3.24) noted the Diatessaron was widely used in the Syrian Church. Clement of Alexandria referred to Odes of Solomon in Stromata (6.13).
Western Church Shepherd of Hermas, 1 Clement Tertullian: On Prescription Against Heretics (22), Clement of Rome: 1 Clement (47.1–3) affirmed these as authoritative.
Egyptian Church Epistle of Barnabas, Gospel of Thomas Clement of Alexandria: Stromata (2.6, 2.7, 2.20) cited the Epistle of Barnabas and Origen appreciated the Gospel of Thomas in his Commentary on John.
Ethiopian Church 1 Enoch, Jubilees, Sinodos, Clement writings Eusebius: Ecclesiastical History (3.25), mentioned these texts in the Ethiopian tradition. Clement of Alexandria: Stromata (6.13) referred to Clement’s writings, but none of these were widely accepted across all Churches.
