Lord's Day SEQUENCE

There were nine primary activities that occurred in sequence on the Lord's Day: the Assembly for Worship, Scripture Readings, Teaching, Hymns and Singing, Communal Prayer, Confession and Repentance, the Eucharist, Offering for the Needy, and Closing Hymns with Dismissal.

This researched article provides a composite view of early Christian (Pre-325 AD) worship practices primarily from the Mediterranean Basin, encompassing regions such as Rome, North Africa, Asia Minor, and Syria, reflecting a blend of local traditions and apostolic teachings.

1. Assembly for Worship

Baptized Christians, including men, women, and children, gathered on Sunday, known as the "Lord’s Day," to celebrate Jesus’ resurrection. This practice originated in the New Testament (1st century), where believers met on the first day of the week to break bread, as recorded in Acts 20:7, 1 Corinthians 16:2, and Revelation 1:10.

Order and Unity in Worship

In the First Epistle of Clement, Clement notes the importance of order and unity in Christian gatherings. He encourages believers to come together in harmony, reflecting the peace and order of God’s creation. This sense of unity was vital for maintaining the integrity of the community. Similarly, in the Epistle of Polycarp to the Philippians, Polycarp urges believers to remain steadfast in their faith and to gather regularly for worship, prayer, and mutual encouragement.

Pliny the Younger’s Observations

The Roman magistrate Pliny the Younger, in his Epistles (10.96), provides an outsider’s perspective on early Christian practices. He notes that Christians met before dawn on a fixed day (Sunday), singing hymns to Christ and committing to ethical practices, such as avoiding fraud, theft, and adultery. These gatherings were marked by a solemn oath to live upright lives, a commitment to Christ’s teachings. Pliny also observed that Christianity had spread widely, affecting people of all ranks, ages, and genders, not only in cities but also in villages and rural areas.

The Setting of Gatherings

Gatherings took place in private homes or informal spaces, as there were no dedicated church buildings at the time. Justin Martyr, in his First Apology (67), describes these meetings as intimate and communal.

The primary purpose of these gatherings was to worship Christ, share in the Eucharist, and encourage one another in faith. The Epistle of Barnabas captures the joy of these meetings, stating: "Wherefore also we keep the eighth day [Sunday] for rejoicing, in the which also Jesus rose from the dead, and having been manifested ascended into the heavens." (15:9).

2. Scripture Readings

The Role of the Reader

The reader (often referred to as a "lector") was a designated individual responsible for proclaiming Scripture aloud during the Lord's Day gatherings The position of lector was recognized as an official role in the community, and it was considered both a practical and spiritual function (Tertullian, De Praescriptione, c. 200; Hippolytus, Apostolic Tradition, c. 215).

The lector's task was to read as long as time permitted. This ensured that the congregation, including those who were illiterate, could hear and understand the Scriptures (Justin Martyr, First Apology 1.67).

The lector was viewed as ranking among the inspired orders, comparable to the prophets, which emphasized the sacred nature of this role. Ordination prayers specifically mentioned the lector's mission to make the Scriptures comprehensible and spiritually meaningful to the gathered assembly (Didascalia Apostolorum 2.20; Apostolic Constitutions 8.22).

The practice of public reading is rooted in the New Testament, as seen in Colossians 4:16, where Paul instructs that his letters be read in different churches, and in 1 Thessalonians 5:27, where he charges the community to ensure his letter is read to all believers.

Content of the Readings

According to Justin Martyr, in his First Apology (67), he states on the content: "The memoirs of the apostles or the writings of the prophets are read." The readings included few main categories:

  • Memoirs of the Apostles: The Gospels recounted the life, teachings, death, and resurrection of Jesus in the books of Matthew, Mark, and Luke.
  • The Writings of the Prophets: Selections from the Old Testament, particularly the prophetic books that foretold the coming of Christ. Passages from prophets like Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Daniel were read.
  • Apostolic letters: (e.g., Paul’s epistles)

3. Teaching (Sermon)

The sermon occurred immediately after the scripture readings, forming a seamless transition from the proclamation of the Word to its interpretation and application.

Who Delivered the Sermon?

The bishop or presbyter (also called the "president of the assembly") was responsible for delivering the sermon. This role was central to the liturgical leadership of the community. The bishop, as the chief pastor, was seen as the spiritual authority and teacher, often described as the "image of Christ" for the congregation [Ignatius of Antioch, Letter to the Magnesians 6:1].

In some cases, prophets or teachers with recognized spiritual gifts also contributed to the teaching, though their roles were increasingly overseen by the bishop to maintain doctrinal unity [Didache 11-13; 1 Corinthians 12:28].

What Was the Content of the Sermon?

The sermon was an exposition of the scripture readings that had just been proclaimed. The leader explained the meaning of the texts, often drawing connections between the Old Testament prophecies and their fulfillment in Christ, as well as applying the teachings to the daily lives of the congregation [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67].

The sermon aimed to instruct believers in the faith, encourage them in their spiritual journey, and exhort them to live according to Christ’s teachings. It often included moral exhortations, calls to repentance, and reminders of the hope of eternal life [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67; 2 Timothy 4:2].

Justin Martyr describes this practice in his First Apology (67): "When the reader has ceased, the president verbally instructs, and exhorts to the imitation of these good things" [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67].

Why Was the Sermon Important?

The sermon served multiple purposes:

  • Instruction: It provided theological and ethical teaching, helping believers understand the scriptures and their relevance to their lives [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67; 2 Timothy 3:16-17].
  • Encouragement: It strengthened the faith of the community, especially in the face of persecution and challenges [Hebrews 10:24-25].
  • Exhortation: It called believers to live holy lives, reflecting the teachings of Christ in their actions and relationships [Titus 2:11-12].

The sermon was also a means of preserving doctrinal unity and countering heresies, as the bishop or presbyter ensured that the teachings aligned with apostolic tradition [Ignatius of Antioch, Letter to the Ephesians 6:1].

How Was the Sermon Delivered?

The leader typically spoke extemporaneously, relying on the guidance of the Holy Spirit and their knowledge of scripture. However, some sermons may have been prepared in advance, especially for special occasions or to address specific issues within the community. The style of preaching was often expository, focusing on explaining the meaning of the scriptures, and paraenetic, highlighting the need for moral exhortation and practical application. The sermon included:

  • Narrative elements: Recounting the acts of God in salvation history [Acts 7:2-53; Hebrews 11:1-40].
  • Theological reflections: Explaining the nature of God, Christ, and the Holy Spirit [John 1:1-18; Colossians 1:15-20].
  • Ethical instructions: Calling believers to live in love, humility, and holiness [Ephesians 4:1-3; 1 Peter 1:15-16].

The congregation responded with "Amen" to affirm the message, as described by Justin Martyr: "When the president has given thanks, and all the people have expressed their assent, saying Amen..." [Justin Martyr, First Apology 65].

4. Hymns and Singing

What Was Sung?

The congregation sang hymns to Christ, including:

Psalms: Early Christians sang psalms from Jewish tradition, reinterpreting them through the lens of Christ’s life and mission. They were encouraged to worship with “psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs,” making melody in their hearts to the Lord (Ephesians 5:19, LSV).

Early Christian Hymns: New Testament passages like Philippians 2:6–11, describing Christ’s humility and equality with God, and Colossians 1:15–20, proclaiming Him as the image of the invisible God and the firstborn over creation, are thought to be early hymns sung by the church.

Spiritual Songs: These were Spirit-inspired songs, either spontaneous or composed, used to praise God, give thanks, and share theological truths. As 1 Corinthians 14:26 notes, gatherings included hymns, teachings, and revelations. Pliny the Younger also observed that Christians sang hymns to Christ as to a god (Epistles 10.96).

How Was the Singing Conducted?

  • Congregations sang hymns to Christ, often meeting before dawn to sing responsively a hymn to Christ as to a god (Pliny the Younger, Epistles 10.96, c. 112 AD).
  • Singing was characterized by simplicity, typically performed a cappella without instrumental accompaniment (Aniol, 2015, What Was Early Church Worship Music Like?).
  • The hymns were chanted or sung in a melodic style, reflecting the musical traditions of the time (Aniol, 2015, What Was Early Church Worship Music Like?).
  • Antiphonal singing, where two groups alternated, was a common practice, as encouraged in Ephesians 5:19 and Colossians 3:16. This method, rooted in Jewish worship, involved alternating groups singing or reciting psalms or hymns (Aniol, 2015, What Was Early Church Worship Music Like?).
  • The focus was on communal participation and the expression of theological truths through music (Aniol, 2015, What Was Early Church Worship Music Like?).

5. Communal Prayer

What Were the Prayers About?

  • The unity and growth of the church [John 17:20-23; Ephesians 4:3-6].
  • Civil authorities and the well-being of society [1 Timothy 2:1-2].
  • Those in need, such as the sick, the poor, and the persecuted [James 5:13-16; Acts 12:5].
  • The community offered prayers of gratitude for God’s blessings, including creation, redemption, and daily provisions [Psalm 100:4; 1 Thessalonians 5:18; Didache 10:3].
  • The congregation praised God for His holiness, power, and love, often using scriptural language and imagery [Psalm 145:1-3; Revelation 4:8; Justin Martyr, First Apology 67].
  • Believers asked for God’s guidance, protection, and strength to live according to His will [Matthew 6:9-13; Philippians 4:6-7].
Justin Martyr, in his First Apology (67), describes these prayers as part of the Sunday worship service: We all rise together and pray"* [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67]. Tertullian, in his Apology (39), also notes that Christians prayed for the emperor, the government, and the world.


6. Confession and Repentance

Confession and repentance occurred before the Eucharist during the gathering. The purpose was to ensure participants approached the Eucharist with a pure heart, in alignment with Jesus' teachings on reconciliation This placement emphasized the need for spiritual and communal preparation before offering or receiving the sacred elements (Didache 14:1; Hermas, Vision II)​

Members of the congregation confessed their sins. This included individuals who had committed minor sins and those guilty of more serious offenses. Confession was both a personal and communal act, reflecting the gravity of sin and the need for reconciliation (Shepherd of Hermas, Vision II, 6.4; O'Donnell, 1908, Penance in the Early Church).

The act involved acknowledging transgressions to reconcile with God and the community. The Didache (14:1) instructs believers to "confess your transgressions, so that your sacrifice may be pure." While minor sins were addressed individually or through prayer, grave sins required public acknowledgment and acts of repentance (Penance in the Early Church, p. 46; The Ministry in the Primitive Church, Knox) ​

Minor Sins:  Minor sins, such as idle talk, envy, or negligence in duties, were confessed privately or during moments of silent prayer, often guided by personal reflection. These sins did not sever one’s relationship with God but required repentance for spiritual growth (Didache, 14:1, c. 100 AD).

Grave Sins: Grave sins, including apostasy, murder, and adultery, were considered severe transgressions that disrupted both the individual’s relationship with God and the unity of the community. They required public confession and acts of penance to demonstrate genuine repentance. Acts such as wearing sackcloth, fasting, or making public gestures of humility were common practices. Tertullian described this in On Purity: “In the church, a sinner is encouraged to publicly acknowledge their sin, that they might be reconciled to God” (Tertullian, On Purity, c. 200 AD).

Mediation by Leaders: A bishop or presbyter played a central role in guiding the penitent through the reconciliation process. This involved ensuring the sincerity of repentance and mediating between the individual and the congregation. As noted in Penance in the Early Church, these leaders bore the responsibility of determining appropriate penances and facilitating the restoration of the sinner to the community (O'Donnell, Penance in the Early Church, 1908, p. 54).


7. The Eucharist (Lord’s Supper)

The Eucharist took place after the prayers, following the sequence of scripture readings, sermon, and intercessions [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67].

Preparation of the elements

Members of the community brought bread and wine (sometimes mixed with water) to the gathering. These elements were placed on a table or altar in the meeting space [Justin Martyr, First Apology 65; Didache 9:1–3].

Dismissal of the Catechumens

Before the Eucharist began, unbaptized individuals (catechumens) were dismissed. Only baptized members of the community were permitted to participate in this sacred meal [Justin Martyr, First Apology 65; Hippolytus, Apostolic Tradition 21].

Prayers of Thanksgiving (Eucharistic Prayer)

The bishop or presider stood before the congregation and offered a prayer of thanksgiving (Greek: eucharistia) [Justin Martyr, First Apology 65–67; Didache 9:2–4; Hippolytus, Apostolic Tradition 4]. This prayer included:

  • Thanksgiving for creation and God’s gifts.
  • Remembrance of Christ’s life, death, and resurrection.
  • Invocation of the Holy Spirit to sanctify the bread and wine, making them the body and blood of Christ.

Consecration of the Elements

The bishop or presider consecrated the bread and wine by speaking the words of institution: "This is my body... This is my blood..." (1 Corinthians 11:23–26). This act was understood to transform the elements into the body and blood of Christ [Justin Martyr, First Apology 66; Ignatius of Antioch, Epistle to the Smyrnaeans 7].

Distribution of the Elements

The consecrated bread and wine were distributed to the congregation. Deacons or other assistants often helped with this process, ensuring that all baptized members received the elements [Justin Martyr, First Apology 65; Hippolytus, Apostolic Tradition 21]. The congregation received the bread and wine as a sacred meal, participating in the body and blood of Christ [1 Corinthians 10:16–17].

Communal Participation

They acknowledged their transgressions before participating in the Eucharist (*Didache* 14:1).

The congregation partook of the bread and wine together, expressing their unity as the body of Christ. This act symbolized their shared faith and mutual love [1 Corinthians 10:17; Ignatius of Antioch, Epistle to the Ephesians 13:1].

Closing Prayer

After the distribution, the bishop or presider offered a final prayer, often asking for God’s blessing on the community and their mission in the world [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67; Didache 10:5].

Collection for the Needy

Following the Eucharist, a collection was taken for the poor, widows, orphans, and others in need. This practice reflected the early Church’s commitment to charity and social justice [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67; Didache 13:4–7].


8. Collection of Offerings for the Needy

The collection occurred after the Eucharistic celebration (1 Corinthians 16:1-2; Acts 20:7). All members of the congregation, particularly those "who prosper and are willing," contributed to the collection (Justin Martyr, First Apology, c. 160 AD). 

Contributions were used to support orphans, widows, the sick, prisoners, and travelers. These funds provided food, shelter, and other necessities, embodying Christian charity (Justin Martyr, First Apology, ch. 67).

Donations were voluntary and proportional to each person's ability, with no compulsion to give (Tertullian, Apology, c. 200 AD).

Offerings were deposited with the "president" (bishop or presbyter) who oversaw their distribution (Justin Martyr, First Apology, ch. 67). Funds were collected and entrusted to the presiding leader (bishop), who distributed them according to need. This included aiding widows, orphans, prisoners, and travelers (Justin Martyr, First Apology, ch. 67).

9. Hymns and Dismissal

The congregation participated in hymns as a communal act of worship, singing songs often derived from scriptural themes and focused on Christ’s resurrection. These hymns fostered unity, joy, and a shared sense of purpose among the believers (Justin Martyr, First Apology 1.67).

Following the hymns, the presiding leader, typically a bishop or elder, delivered a final prayer or blessing to conclude the assembly. This prayer expressed gratitude to God, invoked divine favor, and prepared the congregation to transition from communal worship to daily life (The Lord’s Day: Precedents and Practice).

The benediction emphasized peace, strength, and continued spiritual growth, sending the congregation forth with God’s blessings. Positioned at the conclusion of the Lord’s Day assembly, this practice marked the formal end of worship and noted the importance of integrating faith into everyday living (Justin Martyr, First Apology 1.67; The Lord’s Day: Precedents and Practice).
