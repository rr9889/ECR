Lord's Day

Introduction

The first Christians gathered in homes, sharing meals and celebrating the Eucharist. Over time, these practices evolved into structured liturgies (The legalization of Christianity under Constantine in 313 AD, and the establishment as the state religion of the Roman Empire in 380 AD).

Pre-Nicene Worship practices were rooted in the communal and liturgical traditions of the apostolic era, blending Jewish liturgical customs with distinct Christian theological emphases.

The sources that attest to such a structure can be found in The New Testament (1st Century), The Didache (Late 1st–Early 2nd Century), Pliny the Younger (Early 2nd Century),  Justin Martyr (Mid-2nd Century), The Epistle of Barnabas (Early 2nd Century),  Ignatius of Antioch (Early 2nd Century), Hippolytus of Rome (Early 3rd Century), Tertullian (Late 2nd–Early 3rd Century).

Pliny the Younger, in his Epistles (10.96), describes Christians meeting before dawn on a fixed day, singing hymns to Christ, and binding themselves to ethical commitments, such as avoiding fraud, theft, adultery, and breaking promises.

Gathering of the Community

Baptized Christians, including men, women, and children, gathered on Sunday, known as the "Lord’s Day," to celebrate Jesus’ resurrection. This practice originated in the New Testament (1st century), where believers met on the first day of the week to break bread, as recorded in Acts 20:7. Pliny the Younger, in his Epistles (10.96), notes that Christians met before dawn on a fixed day, singing hymns to Christ and committing to ethical practices, such as avoiding fraud, theft, and adultery.

These gatherings took place in private homes or informal spaces, as there were no dedicated church buildings at the time, as described by Justin Martyr in his First Apology (67). The primary purpose of these meetings was to commemorate Jesus’ resurrection and to strengthen the unity of the Christian community.

Christians came together joyfully, as the Epistle of Barnabas states, "We keep the eighth day [Sunday] with joy" (15:9). This weekly assembly was a central practice, uniting believers in faith and fellowship, and celebrating Christ’s victory over death.

Scripture Readings

A designated reader proclaimed the texts aloud to the congregation. This role ensured that the scriptures were heard and understood by all members of the community, regardless of their ability to read. The practice of public reading is rooted in the New Testament, where Paul instructs that his letters be read aloud to the entire church. For example, in Colossians 4:16, Paul writes, "After this letter has been read to you, see that it is also read in the church of the Laodiceans, and that you in turn read the letter from Laodicea." Similarly, in 1 Thessalonians 5:27, Paul charges the community, "I charge you before the Lord to have this letter read to all the brothers and sisters."

The readings included the "memoirs of the apostles," likely the Gospels, and the writings of the prophets from the Old Testament. Justin Martyr, in his First Apology (67), specifically mentions that "the memoirs of the apostles or the writings of the prophets are read." This practice reflects the continuity with Jewish synagogue worship, where readings from the Law (Torah) and the Prophets were central. The New Testament also emphasizes the importance of scripture, as seen in 1 Timothy 4:13, where Paul instructs Timothy, "Devote yourself to the public reading of Scripture, to preaching, and to teaching."

The readings were conducted in private homes or informal gathering spaces, as there were no dedicated church buildings at the time. Justin Martyr, in his First Apology (67), confirms that early Christians met in such spaces. This practice is consistent with the New Testament, where believers gathered in homes for worship, as seen in passages like Romans 16:5, where Paul greets the church that meets in Priscilla and Aquila’s house.

The primary purpose of the scripture readings was to instruct and remind the community of Christ’s teachings and God’s promises. They provided the basis for the sermon and communal prayers that followed, helping believers grow in their faith. The readings also connected the community to the broader narrative of salvation history, linking Old Testament prophecies to their fulfillment in Christ. This purpose is echoed in the New Testament, where Paul writes in 2 Timothy 3:16, "All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting, and training in righteousness."

The reader stood and proclaimed the texts aloud from scrolls or codices. This practice, inherited from Jewish synagogue worship, ensured that the scriptures were accessible to all, regardless of literacy. Justin Martyr, in his First Apology (67), emphasizes that these readings were central to the liturgy and were followed by a sermon expounding on the texts. The New Testament also highlights the importance of public reading, as seen in Revelation 1:3, which states, "Blessed is the one who reads aloud the words of this prophecy, and blessed are those who hear it and take to heart what is written in it."

Sermon

The bishop or presbyter (president of the assembly) delivered a sermon. Justin Martyr states:

"When the reader has finished, the president verbally instructs and exhorts to the imitation of these good things."

- Justin Martyr, First Apology 67

The sermon served to instruct and encourage believers in their faith, applying the scripture readings to their lives.

Communal Prayer

The congregation prayed together, often standing and facing east. Tertullian writes:

"We pray looking toward the east, where the heaven of heavens is located."

- Tertullian, Apology 16

Prayers included intercessions for the church, the world, and those in need. Justin Martyr notes that these prayers were offered "with one heart and one voice" [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67].

Gathering of the Community

Baptized Christians, including men, women, and children, gathered together to worship and celebrate the resurrection of Jesus. They assembled on Sunday, referred to as the "Lord’s Day," often early in the morning [Pliny the Younger, Epistles 10.96; Epistle of Barnabas 15:9]. Meetings took place in private homes or other informal gathering spaces, since dedicated church buildings did not yet exist [Justin Martyr, First Apology 67]. The purpose of these gatherings was to commemorate Jesus’ resurrection and to strengthen the bonds of the Christian community.

"They met on a fixed day before dawn and sang responsively a hymn to Christ as to a god, bound themselves with an oath—not to some crime—but not to commit fraud, theft, or adultery, not to break a promise, and not to deny a deposit when called upon. After this, it was their custom to depart and to assemble again to partake of food—ordinary and innocent food."

- Pliny the Younger, Epistles 10.96

"We keep the eighth day [Sunday] with joy, the day on which Jesus also rose from the dead."

- Epistle of Barnabas 15:9

Eucharist

The central act of worship was the Eucharist. Justin Martyr describes the process:

"Bread and wine and water are brought, and the president offers prayers and thanksgivings according to his ability. There is a distribution to each, and a participation of that over which thanks have been given."

- Justin Martyr, First Apology 65–67

The Didache provides a Eucharistic prayer:

"We thank You, our Father, for the holy vine of David Your servant, which You made known to us through Jesus Your servant."

- Didache 9:2

The Eucharist was understood as the body and blood of Christ. Ignatius of Antioch writes:

"Let that be considered a valid Eucharist which is celebrated by the bishop or by one whom he appoints."

- Ignatius of Antioch, Epistle to the Smyrnaeans 8

Only those who were baptized and living in accordance with Christian teachings could partake in the Eucharist.

Worship typically began with scripture readings from the "memoirs of the apostles" (likely the Gospels) and the prophets, a practice inherited from Jewish synagogue worship. These readings formed the foundation for teaching and reflection. A sermon followed, delivered by a bishop or presbyter, expounding on the texts and applying them to the lives of the congregation. This emphasized the didactic purpose of worship, instructing believers in the faith. The congregation then offered communal prayers, standing and facing east to symbolize the expectation of Christ’s return. These prayers, offered "with one heart and one voice," included intercessions for the church, the world, and those in need. The central act of worship was the Eucharist, where bread and wine were consecrated by the bishop through prayers of thanksgiving and shared among the baptized. Justin specifies that only those living in accordance with Christian teachings could partake, as the elements were understood as the body and blood of Christ.




Early Christian worship was simple and communal. Services began with scripture readings and sermons, followed by prayers and the Eucharist. Justin Martyr, in the mid-2nd century, described this structure: readings from the apostles' memoirs and prophets, a sermon, communal prayers, and the sharing of bread and wine.

Baptism was a key initiation rite. Candidates renounced Satan, professed faith, and were baptized naked in water. After baptism, they were clothed in white robes, anointed with oil, and participated in the Eucharist. This symbolized their new life in Christ.

Early Churches

Early Christians met in private homes, where they sang hymns, read scriptures, and commemorated the Last Supper. These gatherings were informal, often with animals present. The first churches were simple, built of heavy stones with few windows, and sometimes included defensive towers due to persecution.

The earliest known church, built in the late 3rd century in Aila (modern-day Aqaba, Jordan), featured a central nave, side aisles, and an altar. The Dura-Europos church in Syria, dating to the mid-3rd century, is one of the earliest identified Christian house churches.


The Eucharist was central to early Christian liturgy. Justin Martyr described it as a thank offering, with the president offering prayers and thanksgivings, and the congregation responding with "Amen." The bread and wine were distributed to all, and deacons carried the consecrated elements to those unable to attend.

Fasting was also important, especially before Easter. Initially, the fast lasted one day but was later extended to 40 hours, symbolizing Jesus' 40 days in the wilderness. Sunday, the "little Easter," was a day of joy, often preceded by fasting on Wednesday and Friday.

Conclusion

Early Christian worship was deeply communal, centered on the Eucharist, and marked by simplicity. Over time, as Christianity spread, worship practices became more structured, laying the foundation for the liturgy that would shape Christian worship for centuries.
