Head Coverings

Introduction

The practice of head coverings, or veiling, in early Christianity emerged from the teachings of the Apostle Paul in the 1st century. Paul’s instructions on this practice are found in his epistle to the Corinthians, which appears in the New Testament as 1 Corinthians 11.

Thus, early Christian communities, following Paul’s instruction, required women to pray with their heads covered as a symbol of honor, authority, and alignment with divine order, while men were to pray uncovered.

Early Christian leaders, collectively known as the Church Fathers, addressed and affirmed these practices in their writings from the 2nd and 3rd centuries onward. Christian artwork from the 2nd, 3rd, 4th centuries, and beyond, depicting women wearing veils, further confirms the widespread practice. This evidence highlights the consistency of the practice across geographical, cultural, and theological contexts over time.

Background

Paul wrote about 14 epistles to address various challenges and issues within early Christian communities across regions such as Rome, Corinth, Galatia, Ephesus, Philippi, Colossae, and Thessalonica. He also wrote personal letters to individuals like Timothy, Titus, and Philemon, offering guidance, encouragement, and instruction to strengthen their faith and leadership.

Paul had previously written to the Corinthians, addressing concerns such as sexual immorality. Although this first letter is now lost, Paul references it in his later correspondence. Canonically, the subsequent epistle, designated as First Corinthians by the compilers of the biblical canon, contains specific instructions regarding head coverings for women. This letter was likely composed between A.D. 50 and 58, during Paul’s ministry in Ephesus.

Intended Audience

Modern interpretations of 1 Corinthians 11 often mistakenly assume its instructions were only relevant to the Corinthian church. However, Paul intended his letters to be circulated among other Christian communities.

1. This is evident in the beginning of his first epistle, namely 1 Corinthians 1:2, where Paul addresses not only to the church in Corinth, but also to all church bodies:

2. To the assembly of God that is in Corinth, to those sanctified in Christ Jesus, called saints, with all those calling on the Name of our Lord Jesus Christ in every place—both theirs and ours:

2. Furthermore, it is evidenced in Colossians 4:16, Paul explicitly instructs the Colossians to share his letter with the church in Laodicea and to read the letter from Laodicea in return:

16. And when this letter may be read with you, cause that it may be read also in the assembly of the Laodiceans, and that you also may read that from Laodicea.

The notion that early Christians did not circulate Paul’s teachings is inconsistent with historical evidence. Paul’s letters were preserved precisely because they were widely circulated. Though addressed to specific churches, they were shared and read aloud in other congregations, as seen in Colossians 4:16. This practice ensured their spread and preservation. Furthermore, the idea that early churches operated in isolation is unsupported. Paul’s description on how the church should operate is as follows: "one body in Christ" (Romans 12:5).

Analysis

Verse-by-verse analysis of 1 Corinthians 11

Verse 2: Praise for Faithfulness

Paul praises the Corinthians for remembering his teachings and adhering to them.

2. And I praise you, brothers, that in all things you remember me, and according as I delivered to you, you keep the deliverances.

Verses 5-6: Instructions on Head Coverings

Paul instructs that men should pray uncovered, while women should pray covered, as a matter of honor.

5. Every man praying or prophesying, having the head covered, dishonors his head, 6. and every woman praying or prophesying with the head uncovered, dishonors her own head, for it is one and the same thing with her being shaven. For if a woman is not covered—then let her be shorn, and if it is a shame for a woman to be shorn or shaven—let her be covered.

Verses 7-9: Creation Order

Paul explains that man is the image and glory of God, while woman is the glory of man, reflecting the creation order.

7. For a man, indeed, ought not to cover the head, being the image and glory of God, and a woman is the glory of a man, 8. for a man is not of a woman, but a woman is of a man, 9. for also was a man not created because of the woman, but a woman because of the man.

Verse 10: Authority and Angels

Paul states that women should have a symbol of authority on their heads because of the angels.

10. Because of this, the woman ought to have [a token of] authority on the head, because of the messengers.

Verses 11-12: Mutual Dependence

Paul emphasizes that men and women are interdependent and equal in the Lord.

11. But neither is a man apart from a woman, nor a woman apart from a man, in the Lord. 12. For as the woman is of the man, so also the man is through the woman, and all things are of God.

Verse 13: Self-Judgment

Paul asks the Corinthians to judge for themselves whether it is proper for a woman to pray uncovered.

13. Judge in your own selves: is it seemly for a woman to pray to God uncovered?

Verses 14-15: Nature's Teaching

Paul appeals to nature, stating that long hair is a dishonor for men but a glory for women, serving as a natural covering.

14. Does not even nature itself teach you that if a man indeed has long hair, it is a dishonor to him? 15. And a woman, if she has long hair, it is a glory to her, because the hair has been given to her instead of a covering.

Verse 16: No Contention

Paul concludes by stating that the custom of head coverings is not up for debate, as it is a practice upheld by the churches of God.

16. And if anyone thinks to be contentious, we have no such custom, neither the assemblies of God.

Practice Among Communities

Regions

During the first three centuries of Christianity, the practice of women wearing head coverings became an established norm in key regions, including Rome, Antioch, and Carthage in Africa.REF Johnson, Lewis. (1962). The Wycliffe Bible Commentary. Chicago: Moody Press. pp. 1247–1248.

Artwork

Artistic evidence shows women wearing head coverings during worship. In the 1st century, frescoes in the Roman catacombs of Priscilla and Domitilla depict veiled women praying. By the 3rd century, mosaics in Dura-Europos, Syria, show veiled women in worship. In the 4th century, Christian sarcophagi in Rome, like the Sarcophagus of the Good Shepherd, feature veiled women in biblical scenes, while frescoes in Ephesus and Cappadocia catacombs depict veiled figures, including Mary, in worship.REF 3rd Century

Early Christian leaders, including Clement of Alexandria, Hippolytus of Rome, Irenaeus of Lyons, John Chrysostom, and Tertullian, universally agreed on the necessity of a cloth veil for women.REF Clement of Alexandria. Regarding the material, the veil must be made of substantial, opaque cloth (e.g., wool or thick linen)—not thin linen, transparent fabrics, or decorative materials. Turbans, woollen bands, and small coifs are also rejected as inadequate by most early Christian leaders, including Clement of Alexandria, Hippolytus of Rome, Tertullian, and John Chrysostom.REF Clement of Alexandria. Paedagogus, Book 2, Chapter 11; Hippolytus of Rome. Apostolic Tradition, Chapter 18; Tertullian. On the Veiling of Virgins, Chapter 17; John Chrysostom. Homilies on 1 Corinthians, Homily 26.

Regarding coverage, the veil must fully cover the head and neck, extending to where the robe begins. Partial coverings, such as fringes, tufts, or coifs, are criticized as insufficient by Tertullian, Hippolytus, and Chrysostom. Tertullian describes how some women use turbans or headbands (woollen bands), covering only the front of the head, or small linen coifs that barely cover the top, stopping short of the ears. He argues these fail to provide full modesty and insists the veil must mimic the natural coverage of unbound hair, draping over the head and neck.REF Tertullian. On the Veiling of Virgins, Chapter 17; Hippolytus of Rome. Apostolic Tradition, Chapter 18; John Chrysostom. Homilies on 1 Corinthians, Homily 26.

In the Dura-Europos (Syrian) artwork, it depicts a wedding scene in which each figure wears a long-sleeved white chiton, belted at the waist, paired with a long white veil. The figures are portrayed with heroic, androgynous bodies, their flowing hair covered by veils, and dressed in loose, unrestrained garments, predominantly white, symbolizing purity and grace.REF Peppard, M. (5678). The World’s Oldest Church: Bible, Art, and Ritual at Dura-Europos, Syria. Yale University Press.

Despite differences in time, geography, and cultural nuances, early Christian clothing standards for women—particularly the use of veils—remained consistent as an expression of religious modesty, purity, and grace.

When to veil?

The practice of women wearing veils is specifically tied to public worship, not everyday life. Johnson notes that Paul regarded the Corinthian practice of women worshiping bareheaded as “irreligious rather than indecorous”, noting that the issue was not about social custom but about proper reverence in worship. This interpretation is supported by well-respected scholars such as Leon Morris, who highlights the theological significance of the veil in public worship, and Gordon D. Fee, who argues that Paul’s instructions were rooted in spiritual principles rather than cultural norms. Additionally, F.F. Bruce and David E. Garland affirm that the veil symbolized the woman’s acknowledgment of the divine order in the context of public worship.REF Johnson, L. Lewis. (1962). The Wycliffe Bible Commentary. Chicago: Moody Press. pp. 1247–1248; Morris, L. (1985). The First Epistle of Paul to the Corinthians. Tyndale New Testament Commentaries; Fee, G. D. (1987). The First Epistle to the Corinthians. NICNT; Bruce, F. F. (1971). 1 and 2 Corinthians. New Century Bible Commentary; Garland, D. E. (2003). 1 Corinthians. Baker Exegetical Commentary on the New Testament.

Early Christian Leaders on Head Coverings

There is a clear consensus among early Christian writers that head coverings are to be observed. The literature affirming this practice spans a wide geographical area, diverse theological perspectives, and an extensive time period, from the 1st to the 5th century.

Here are some of the earliest and respected theologians' quotations on the matter:

Clement of Alexandria (c. 150–215 AD)

Christian apologist, presbyter, and theologian who served as the second leader of the Catechetical School of Alexandria. In his work  The Instructor, Book III, Chapter 11 (Paedagogus), written around 198 AD, he wrote:

"Woman and man are to go to church ... Let the woman observe this, further. Let her be entirely covered, unless she happen to be at home. ... And she will never fall, who puts before her eyes modesty, and her shawl ... For this is the wish of the Word, since it is becoming for her to pray veiled."

Tertullian (c. 160–240 AD)

Early Christian author from Carthage, was the first to produce extensive Latin Christian literature. A prominent apologist and polemicist, he addressed head coverings for women in his work On the Veiling of Virgins, Chapter 9 (c. 200 AD):

"Is the reason she is allowed to go without a veil so that she can stand out and be noticed as she enters the church? Is it so she can show off her holiness by leaving her head uncovered? If she deserves recognition, wouldn’t it be better to honor her with some kind of leadership role or responsibility, rather than letting her disregard the practice of covering her head?"

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170–235), a prominent bishop and theologian, documented early Christian practices in his work Apostolic Tradition, Chapter 18 (verse 5) (c. 215). He wrote:

"All women should cover their heads with a proper veil, not just a piece of thin linen, as that doesn’t count as a true covering."

Conclusion

The practice of head coverings was widely supported in early Christian writings, showing a strong consensus across different regions and cultures. From the 1st to the 5th century, Christian leaders from places like Alexandria, Carthage, Rome, Caesarea, Antioch, and Hippo spoke of its importance.

These writers came from diverse backgrounds, ranging from the Greek-speaking East to the Latin West, and included theologians, bishops, and monastic leaders. Despite their differences, they all agreed that head coverings were a way to show modesty, humility, and respect for apostolic teachings.

If early Christians followed and affirmed Paul's teachings to the letter, as historical evidence suggests, then the apostle was grounding the principles of head coverings in theology rather than culture. These theological principles include:

  1. Apostle Authority: Paul’s instructions on head coverings carry the weight of apostolic authority, as he explicitly states in verse 2.
  2. Creation Order: Paul references the divine order of creation, the relationship between man, woman, and God.
  3. Observance of Angels: Paul mentions that head coverings are worn "because of the angels," suggesting a spiritual dimension to the practice.
  4. Natural Law: Paul appeals to natural distinctions, arguing that head coverings reflect the inherent order and propriety established by God.
