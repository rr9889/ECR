Head Coverings in Early Christianity

By Ryan Reeves

In the year of our Lord 2025, on the third day of January...

Introduction

The practice of wearing head coverings in early Christianity has its roots in the teachings of the Apostle Paul and reflects both early christian culture. This article explores the biblical foundation, significance, and spiritual meaning of head coverings in the lives of early Christians.

Biblical Basis

The so-called "Pauline epistles" were written to address problems that arose in the Christian communities spanning Rome, Corinth, Galatia, Ephesus, Philippi, Colossae, Thessalonica, and many more. The epistles addressed and provided solutions to theological issues and misunderstandings. For example, he emphasized the centrality of faith in Christ, grace over works, and the unity of Jews and Gentiles in the body of Christ.REF Supporting Evidence:
The Pauline epistles, including Romans, Corinthians, Galatians, Ephesians, Philippians, Colossians, and Thessalonians, were written to address specific issues in early Christian communities. Paul emphasized themes such as faith in Christ (Romans 3:22–24), grace over works (Ephesians 2:8–9), and the unity of Jews and Gentiles in Christ (Galatians 3:28). These letters were instrumental in shaping early Christian theology and practice.

Source:
Carson, D. A., & Moo, Douglas J. (2005). An Introduction to the New Testament. Grand Rapids: Zondervan. pp. 230–350.

In the canonical "First Corinthians" epistle, there lies an instruction regarding head coverings for females. It was likely written around 50 and 58 C.E. while he was in Ephesus.REF Supporting Evidence:
The instruction on head coverings is found in 1 Corinthians 11:2–16, where Paul addresses the practice of women covering their heads during prayer and prophecy.

Historical Context:
The epistle was written during Paul's ministry in Ephesus, likely between 50 and 58 C.E. This period aligns with Paul's efforts to address issues in the Corinthian church, including disputes over worship practices and social conduct.

Analyzing the text

The translation of Paul's words is taken from Literal Standard Version (LSV).

Verse 2: Praise for Faithfulness

Paul praises the Corinthians for remembering his teachings and adhering to them.

2. And I praise you, brothers, that in all things you remember me, and according as I delivered to you, you keep the deliverances.

Verses 5-6: Instructions on Head Coverings

Paul instructs that men should pray uncovered, while women should pray covered, as a matter of honor.

5. Every man praying or prophesying, having the head covered, dishonors his head, 6. and every woman praying or prophesying with the head uncovered, dishonors her own head, for it is one and the same thing with her being shaven. For if a woman is not covered—then let her be shorn, and if it is a shame for a woman to be shorn or shaven—let her be covered.

Verses 7-9: Creation Order

Paul explains that man is the image and glory of God, while woman is the glory of man, reflecting the creation order.

7. For a man, indeed, ought not to cover the head, being the image and glory of God, and a woman is the glory of a man, 8. for a man is not of a woman, but a woman is of a man, 9. for also was a man not created because of the woman, but a woman because of the man.

Verse 10: Authority and Angels

Paul states that women should have a symbol of authority on their heads because of the angels.

10. Because of this, the woman ought to have [a token of] authority on the head, because of the messengers.

Verses 11-12: Mutual Dependence

Paul emphasizes that men and women are interdependent and equal in the Lord.

11. But neither is a man apart from a woman, nor a woman apart from a man, in the Lord. 12. For as the woman is of the man, so also the man is through the woman, and all things are of God.

Verse 13: Self-Judgment

Paul asks the Corinthians to judge for themselves whether it is proper for a woman to pray uncovered.

13. Judge in your own selves: is it seemly for a woman to pray to God uncovered?

Verses 14-15: Nature's Teaching

Paul appeals to nature, stating that long hair is a dishonor for men but a glory for women, serving as a natural covering.

14. Does not even nature itself teach you that if a man indeed has long hair, it is a dishonor to him? 15. And a woman, if she has long hair, it is a glory to her, because the hair has been given to her instead of a covering.

Verse 16: No Contention

Paul concludes by stating that the custom of head coverings is not up for debate, as it is a practice upheld by the churches of God.

16. And if anyone thinks to be contentious, we have no such custom, neither the assemblies of God.

Practice among communities

Regions

During the first three centuries of Christianity, the practice of women wearing head coverings became an established norm in key regions, including Rome, Antioch, and Carthage in Africa.REF Johnson, Lewis. (1962). The Wycliffe Bible Commentary. Chicago: Moody Press. pp. 1247–1248.

Artwork

Artistic evidence shows women wearing head coverings during worship. In the 1st century, frescoes in the Roman catacombs of Priscilla and Domitilla depict veiled women praying. By the 3rd century, mosaics in Dura-Europos, Syria, show veiled women in worship. In the 4th century, Christian sarcophagi in Rome, like the Sarcophagus of the Good Shepherd, feature veiled women in biblical scenes, while frescoes in Ephesus and Cappadocia catacombs depict veiled figures, including Mary, in worship. Mosaics in the Basilica of St. John in Ephesus further confirm this practice.REF 3rd Century

Physical description of head covering

According to early Christian historian David Bercot (1992), historical records indicate that the early churches universally understood Paul to be referring to a cloth veil, not long hair. The practice of Christian women wearing a cloth head covering was consistently taught by the Church Fathers and observed throughout Christian history (p. 68).REF Bercot, David W. (1992). Common Sense: A New Approach to Understanding Scripture. Scroll Publishing Co. p. 68. ISBN 978-0-924722-06-6.

Early Christian figures affirm the practice

There is a clear consensus among early Christian writers that head coverings are to be observed. The literature affirming this practice spans a wide geographical area, diverse theological perspectives, and an extensive time period, from the 1st to the 5th century.

Here are some of the earliest and respected theologians' quotations on the matter:

Clement of Alexandria (c. 150 – c. 215 AD) was a Christian apologist, presbyter in Alexandria, a missionary theologian to the Hellenistic world, and the second known leader and teacher of the Catechetical School of Alexandria. In his work The Instructor (Paedagogus), he wrote:

"Woman and man are to go to church ... Let the woman observe this, further. Let her be entirely covered, unless she happen to be at home. ... And she will never fall, who puts before her eyes modesty, and her shawl ... For this is the wish of the Word, since it is becoming for her to pray veiled."

— The Instructor, Book III, Chapter 11.


Tertullian (c. 160 AD - c. 240 AD) was a early Christian author from Carthage in the Roman province of Africa. He is notable for being the first Christian writer to produce an extensive body of Latin Christian literature. He was also an influential apologist and a fierce polemicist against heresy. In his work On the Veiling of Virgins (circa 200 AD), he addresses the practice of women covering their heads in church:

"Is the reason she is allowed to go without a veil so that she can stand out and be noticed as she enters the church? Is it so she can show off her holiness by leaving her head uncovered? If she deserves recognition, wouldn’t it be better to honor her with some kind of leadership role or responsibility, rather than letting her disregard the practice of covering her head?"

— Tertullian, On the Veiling of Virgins, Chapter 9


Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170 – c. 235) was a bishop of the church of Rome and one of the most significant Christian theologians of the late second and early third centuries. Writing around the year 215, he compiled a detailed record of the customs and practices observed by the generations before him. In his work, Apostolic Tradition, he includes the following statement:

"All women should cover their heads with a proper veil, not just a piece of thin linen, as that doesn’t count as a true covering."

— Hippolytus, Apostolic Tradition, Chapter 18 (verse 5)


The practice of head coverings is widely affirmed across early Christian literature, reflecting a remarkable consensus that transcends geographical, cultural, and theological boundaries. From the 1st to the 5th century, writers from diverse regions—such as Alexandria (Clement), Carthage (Tertullian), Rome (Hippolytus), Caesarea (Origen and Basil), Antioch (John Chrysostom and Theodoret), and Hippo (Augustine)—all emphasize the importance of this practice.

Despite their differing backgrounds and theological emphases, they unanimously uphold the tradition of head coverings as a vital expression of modesty, humility, and adherence to apostolic teaching.
