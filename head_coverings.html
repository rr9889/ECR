Head Coverings

Background

Situation

1 Corinthians was composed between A.D. 54 and 56, during Paul’s ministry in Ephesus (Schreiner, 2018, p. 4). Paul wrote about 14 epistles (Stevens, 2018) to address various challenges and issues within early Christian communities across regions such as Rome, Corinth, Galatia, Ephesus, Philippi, Colossae, and Thessalonica. He wrote to Timothy, Titus, and Philemon, offering guidance and encouragement.

Authoritative or Advise?

At the beginning of the epistle, Paul addresses both the Corinthians and all believers sanctified in Christ “in every place” (1 Cor. 1), showing that his letters were intended for broader circulation among Christian communities (Col. 4:16). He reinforces this by emphasizing that the church should function as “one body in Christ” (Rom. 12:5). Schreiner (2018, p. 20) affirms this, describing Paul’s teachings as extending beyond local concerns. Paul himself viewed his instructions as divinely authoritative, considering them part of the gospel.

Corinth Demographics

The Christians in Corinth consisted of mostly Gentles, although was some Jewish presence (Schreiner, 2018, p. 4). It can be best described as "culturally mixed audience of Christian converts" according to Wilson (2018, p. 136).

Pagan society in Corinth did not mandate head coverings for women. The city blended Jewish, Greek, and Roman customs at a time when Greek and Roman traditions were weakening, contributing to cultural flux and dissolution (Marlowe, 2005).

Paul's Mandate

The Wycliffe Bible Commentary (1962, p. 1229) by S. Lewis Johnson outlines three key reasons for the veiling of women in public worship according to 1 Corinthians 11:2-16: Theological (11:2-6), Biblical (11:7-12), the Physical (11:13-16).

 Commentary

Commendation for Tradition Paul commends the Corinthians for remembering him and holding fast to his teachings, which refer to his inspired instructions, not later church traditions (MacDonald, 2008, pp. 1784-1787).

Divine Hierarchy The consensus ties this to divine order and authority (Eph. 5:23; Gen. 2:22-23; 3:16), though it does not negate equality between men and women (Ellis, 2021, pp. 403, 308, 397).

Men Should Not Cover Their Heads Ambrosiaster (2009, p. 172) states that man, as God's image, should not cover his head, as it would conceal God's glory.

Uncovered Praying or prophesying dishonors
 We should, even in our dress and habit, avoid every thing that may dishonour Christ (Henry, 1706). Covering symbolizes her subjection to her husband and divine order (Chrysostom, 1889).

Shame of an Uncovered Head Stockton (2024, pp. 68-86) notes Paul's conditional statement: If a woman refuses to cover, she should be shorn.

Man as God's Image, Woman as Man’s Glory There is a distance between the glory of God and the glory of a man, for a man is formed in the likeness of God and a woman is not. She is the likeness of God because of the man (Ambrosiaster, 2009).

Woman Created from and for Man Cosmological reasoning: Woman was created for man, not vice versa. This does not imply male superiority but highlights mutual dependence in the Lord, balancing hierarchy (Schreiner, 2018; Stockton, 2024).

Head Covering as a Sign of Authority (Angels) Paul’s mention of "because of the angels" ties head coverings to theological order. Angels are seen as ministering spirits, present in worship, or witnesses to divine order (Ellis, 2021).

Interdependence of Men and Women Findlay (1956, p. 873) notes that Paul’s teaching does not diminish a woman’s value but rather affirms mutual dependence within God’s created order.

Judgment on Proper Worship Conduct Stockton (2024, pp. 68-86) explains that Judge in verse 13 is an urgent command, while comely refers to nature's standard of propriety, requiring discernment.

Nature Teaches Gender Distinctions The Church’s Bible (2005, p. 180) cites John Chrysostom, who argues that nature (God) established gender distinctions, which should not be overturned.

A Woman’s Hair as Her Natural Covering Brown (2017, pp. 1-17) notes that, until the mid-20th century, scholars widely agreed that Paul required a material head covering.

Churches Follow This Custom Paul concludes by stating that if anyone is contentious, there is no other practice in the churches of God (Piper and Grudem, 2021), reinforcing that his instructions are binding across all congregations.

Practice

Geographic Area

During the first three centuries of Christianity, it became customary for women to wear head coverings during worship in prominent Christian centers, such as Rome, Antioch, and Carthage in Africa. Therefore, it was widely embraced and established as a norm within the Church. (Lewis, 1962, pp. 1247–1248).

A Unique Tradition

According to biblical linguist Michael D. Marlowe (2005), Paul’s explanation of head coverings reflects a distinct Christian custom, separate from Jewish, Greek, or Roman traditions. He critiques interpretations that fail to recognize the early Church as a unique subculture with its own practices.

This understanding of head covering as a distinctly Christian ordinance, and timeless practice is affirmed by numerous reputable commentaries, including Edwards (1885, p. 270), MacDonald (1989, p. 1785), Ryrie (1976, p. 303), Zondervan (1957, Vol. II, p. 133), and Barrett (1971, p. 251), among others.

Ben Witherington III (1995, pp. 235–38) explains: "Paul is not simply endorsing standard Roman or even Greco-Roman customs in Corinth. Paul was about the business of reforming his converts' social assumptions and conventions in the context of the Christian community. They were to model new Christian customs, common in the assemblies of God but uncommon in the culture, thus staking out their own sense of a unique identity."

Prominent Early Church Thoughts

The Church Fathers, an influential group of Christian writers from the 1st to 5th centuries, representing diverse regions and theological perspectives, universally agreed that Paul's words referred to veiling as a God-given practice during prayer or prophesying. Figures such as Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, Tertullian, Augustine, John Chrysostom, and Jerome all upheld this interpretation in their writings (Finney, 2010; Stafford, 2024, pp. 4,10; Wilson, 2018, p. 144).

Literature

1st Century

Paul's epistle is the earliest recorded instance of promoting the Christian practice in the first century, therefore establishing a long tradition of women covering their heads in worship (Innes, 2021).

2nd Century

Irenaeus references the practice of veiling when discussing how a certain group misinterpret Scripture. He cites verse 10, “a woman ought to have a veil upon her head, because of the angels.” Irenaeus notes that the group distorted this passage to support their own doctrines (Schaff, 1885/2001, p. 535).

3rd Century

Clement of Alexandria commented "Let the woman observe this, further. Let her be entirely covered, unless she happen to be at home... For this is the wish of the Word, since it is becoming for her to pray veiled” (Schaff, 1885/2001b).

Tertullian affirms that the Corinthians interpreted Paul’s teaching as requiring veiling, a practice they continued in his time. He notes most churches upheld head coverings, though some allowed variation without controversy. He points to apostolic churches as the standard for practice (Bercot, 2021, p. 667).

Hippolytus of Rome, in Apostolic Tradition, wrote: "All the women should cover their heads with a cloak, and not simply with a piece of linen, which is not a proper veil" (Easton, 2014, p. 43).

Artwork

In the first century, Christians had limited access to land and capital, both essential for artistic production (Finney, 1994, p. 108). However, by the second century, frescoes in the Roman catacombs of Priscilla and Domitilla depicted veiled women in prayer. In the third century, mosaics from Dura-Europos, Syria, similarly show veiled women in worship. By the fourth century, Christian sarcophagi in Rome, such as the Sarcophagus of the Good Shepherd, featured veiled women in biblical scenes, reflecting the continuity of this practice in Christian art.

Physical Description

The following synthesis is drawn from the writings of key early Christian figures, including Clement of Alexandria, Hippolytus of Rome, Irenaeus of Lyons, John Chrysostom, and Tertullian.

Correct Practices
  • Use a substantial, opaque cloth – Examples include wool or thick linen.
  • Ensure full coverage – The veil should extend over the head and neck, reaching where the robe begins.
  • Mimic the natural coverage of unbound hair – The veil should drape modestly over the head and neck.

Rejected practices

  • Avoid thin or decorative materials – Transparent fabrics, thin linen, or ornamental veils are discouraged.
  • No partial coverings – Fringes, tufts, or veils that leave part of the head exposed are considered insufficient.
  • Reject alternatives like turbans, woolen bands, or small coifs – Does not provide adequate modesty.
  • Do not use minimal coverings – Headbands, small linen coifs, or anything that stops short of covering the ears are deemed inappropriate.
