Headcoverings

When Modernity Overrules Scripture

The Issue

The practice of head coverings in Christianity is now all but buried, thanks to the cunning deceptions of these wolves in sheep’s clothing—Bethany Verrett of BibleStudyTools, Trey Soto of Crosswalk.com, and the ever-enlightened Got Questions Ministries. With them, being the first results when searching on Google. These self-appointed prophets of modernity, dripping with worldly wisdom, have suddenly unlocked a new revelation that eluded every church father, theologian, and saint for two millennia. How remarkable that, after 2,000 years, they alone have discovered that Paul’s words were merely cultural suggestions—as if the Holy Spirit was bound by time and subject to revision. This so-called new revelation emerged in the mid-20th century, despite prior scholarly consensus that Paul required a material head covering (Brown, 2017, pp. 1-17). Let these individuals be accursed for distorting and adding to God's Word!

Background

The First Epistle of Paul to the Corinthians was composed between A.D. 54 and 56, during Paul’s ministry in Ephesus (Schreiner, 2018, p. 4). Paul wrote about 13 epistles to address various challenges and issues within early Christian communities.

Authoritative or Advise?

At the beginning of the epistle, Paul addresses both the Corinthians and all believers sanctified in Christ “in every place” (1 Cor. 1), furthermore his letters were intended for broad circulation among Christian communities (e.g. Col. 4:16). Paul argues uniformity dispute our differences being “one body in Christ” (Rom. 12:5). Paul extends his instructions beyond Corinth to "all the assemblies" (churches), showing his teachings aren’t local but broadly applicable (1 Cor. 7:17).

Corinth Demographics

The Christians in Corinth consisted of mostly Gentiles, although was some Jewish presence (Schreiner, 2018, p. 4). It can be best described as "culturally mixed audience of Christian converts" according to Wilson (2018, p. 136). Therefore, is it not accurate to say Paul is enforcing an ethnic custom. Furthermore, pagan society in Corinth did not mandate head coverings for women. The city blended Jewish, Greek, and Roman customs at a time when Greek and Roman traditions were weakening, contributing to cultural flux and dissolution (Marlowe, 2005; Pawlak, 2013).

Practice

Geographic Area

In the first three centuries, women in major Christian hubs like Rome, Antioch, and Carthage commonly wore head coverings during worship, making it a widely accepted Church norm (Lewis, 1962, pp. 1247–1248). This practice also echoed a broader custom in the ancient pagan world—spanning the Mediterranean, Greek, Near Eastern, and Roman regions—predating Paul’s instructions (Stafford, 2024, p. 5).

A Unique Tradition

Biblical linguist Michael D. Marlowe (2005) argues that Paul’s head covering instructions in 1 Corinthians 11 reflect a unique Christian practice, distinct from Jewish, Greek, or Roman traditions, and critiques views that overlook the early Church as a distinct subculture. This view of head coverings as a timeless Christian ordinance is supported by commentaries like Edwards (1885, p. 270), MacDonald (1989, p. 1785), Ryrie (1976, p. 303), Zondervan (1957, Vol. II, p. 133), and Barrett (1971, p. 251). Ben Witherington III (1995, pp. 235–236) adds that Paul wasn’t just adopting Roman or Greco-Roman norms but reforming his converts’ social conventions to create a unique Christian identity, distinct from surrounding culture, for the assemblies of God.

Prominent Early Church Thoughts

The Church Fathers, influential Christian writers from the 1st to 5th centuries representing diverse regions and theological perspectives, unanimously interpreted Paul's words as prescribing veiling as a divinely ordained practice during prayer or prophesying. Figures including Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, Tertullian, Augustine, John Chrysostom, and Jerome all maintained this interpretation in their writings (Finney, 2010; Stafford, 2024, pp. 4,10; Wilson, 2018, p. 144).

Paul's epistle established the earliest recorded instance of Christian head covering, initiating a lasting tradition (Innes, 2021). By the 2nd century, Irenaeus referenced veiling when discussing misinterpreted Scripture, specifically citing 1 Corinthians 11:10 (Schaff, 1885/2001, p. 535). In the 3rd century, Clement of Alexandria directed women to be fully veiled during prayer, even within their homes (Schaff, 1885/2001b). Tertullian confirmed that the Corinthians practiced veiling, noting that while most churches maintained this practice, some allowed variations (Bercot, 2021, p. 667). Hippolytus specifically required women to wear cloth coverings rather than mere linen ones (Easton, 2014, p. 43).

Paul's Mandate

The Wycliffe Bible Commentary (1962, p. 1229) outlines three key reasons.

Theological

Paul praises the Corinthians for adhering to his inspired teachings, distinct from later church traditions (MacDonald, 2008, pp. 1784-1787). Scholars link this to divine order and authority (Eph. 5:23; Gen. 2:22-23; 3:16), while affirming gender equality (Ellis, 2021). Ambrosiaster (2009, p. 172) argues man, as God’s image, should not cover his head, lest it hide God’s glory. Our dress should honor Christ (Henry, 1706). A woman’s covering reflects submission to her husband and divine order (Chrysostom, 1889). Stockton (2024, pp. 68-86) highlights Paul’s condition: if a woman won’t cover, she should be shorn.

Biblical

Ambrosiaster (2009) highlights a distinction: man, formed in God’s likeness, reflects His glory directly (1 Cor. 11:7), while woman does so through man (1 Cor. 11:3). Cosmologically, woman was made for man, not vice versa (Gen. 2:18; 1 Cor. 11:9), suggesting a hierarchy yet mutual dependence in the Lord (1 Cor. 11:11-12), not male superiority (Schreiner, 2018). Paul’s “because of the angels” (1 Cor. 11:10) ties head coverings to divine order, with angels as ministering spirits (Heb. 1:14), present in worship, or witnesses to this structure (Ellis, 2021).

Physical

Stockton (2024, pp. 68-86) clarifies that in 1 Corinthians 11:13, "judge" is a strong call to decide, and "proper" means what’s naturally proper, needing careful thought. The Church’s Bible (2005, p. 180) cites John Chrysostom, who argues that nature (God) established gender distinctions (1 Cor. 11:14-15), which should not be overturned. Paul concludes in 1 Corinthians 11:16 that if anyone is contentious, no other practice exists in the churches of God, reinforcing his instructions as binding across all congregations (Piper and Grudem, 2021).

Physical Description

The following synthesis is drawn from the writings of key early Christian figures, including Clement of Alexandria, Hippolytus of Rome, Irenaeus of Lyons, John Chrysostom, and Tertullian.

Category Description
Correct Practices Use a substantial, opaque cloth – Examples include wool or thick linen.
Ensure full coverage – The veil should extend over the head and neck, reaching where the robe begins.
Mimic the natural coverage of unbound hair – The veil should drape modestly over the head and neck.
Rejected Practices Avoid thin or decorative materials – Transparent fabrics, thin linen, or ornamental veils are discouraged.
No partial coverings – Fringes, tufts, or veils that leave part of the head exposed are considered insufficient.
Reject alternatives like turbans, woolen bands, or small coifs – Does not provide adequate modesty.
Do not use minimal coverings – Headbands, small linen coifs, or anything that stops short of covering the ears are deemed inappropriate.
