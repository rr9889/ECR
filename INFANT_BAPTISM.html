Infant Baptism

When Emergencies Become Doctrine

The Issue

Did you know some believe unbaptized babies are denied heaven—sent to hell, placed in Limbo (a widely accepted but unofficial Catholic idea), or deprived of grace for not being baptized in time? Or that baptism removes original sin from Adam and Eve? While once widely accepted, these views lack strong biblical support. The Bible emphasizes God’s justice and mercy, not infant condemnation (Matthew 19:14). So why the rush? God’s grace surpasses human rules. Strict biblical analysis shows baptism is consistently linked to personal faith, repentance, and belief, which infants cannot express. While it may mark Church inclusion, no clear biblical mandate requires infant baptism for salvation, forgiveness, or receiving the Holy Spirit.

First Century

A majority of scholars conclude that infant baptism was not widely practiced in the 1st century due to the absence of clear, explicit evidence (Ferguson, 2009; Wright, 2007; Cross & Livingstone, 2005; Grenz, 2000, p. 528). First-century Christians believed that small children, especially infants, were inherently sinless, so conversion was achieved by personally persuading children rather than through a formal baptismal rite for infants (Keeran, 2009, p. 11). Christianity at this stage, had no specific doctrine of original sin (Obach, 2008, p. 41).
Household Baptisms?

Household baptisms in Acts and 1 Corinthians don’t clearly include infants, as Ferguson (2009, p. 178) argues, calling infant baptism an “argument from silence.” Examples suggest conscious faith: Cornelius’ household “heard the word” and spoke in tongues (Acts 10:44, 46); Lydia’s, a wealthy businesswoman’s, likely had servants and family, with no children noted (Acts 16:14); the Philippian jailer’s household “rejoiced, having believed” (Acts 16:34); Crispus’ household believed and was baptized (Acts 18:8); and Stephanas’ household actively served the church, “devoted to the saints” (1 Cor. 16:15).

Second Century

Early 2nd-century apologists like Aristides (c. 125 AD, Apology 15) and Athenagoras (c. 177 AD, On the Resurrection) suggest infants are sinless, with Aristides noting they die “sinless” and Athenagoras implying they’re not judged, having done “neither evil nor good” (Ferguson, 1999b, pp. 53-54). Justin Martyr (c. 153-155 AD, Second Apology 10; First Apology 15) adds that children are morally neutral until rational choice, praising their purity without innate sin (Wiley, 2002). However, no text confirms conversion by persuasion over baptism. Clement of Rome and Ignatius assume universal sin (Wiley, 2002, pp. 38–39), while Clement of Alexandria hints at inherited sin (The Instructor). Scholars (Ferguson, 2009; Wall, 1703) see 2nd-century infant baptism as rare, often emergency-driven due to high mortality (30%) (Osiek & MacDonald, 2006), with Wright and Aland noting traces by 200 AD (Wright, 2007; Aland, 2005). Epitaphs vary—some suggest emergency baptisms, others don’t (Ferguson, 1999, p. 162; Keeran, 2009, p. 11). Irenaeus (c. 174 AD, Against Heresies II, 22.4) uses symbolic language of Christ sanctifying all ages, not mandating infant baptism, per Jenson (2012) and Meyers (1988), though Bakke (2005) and Nelson (1996) see it as baptismal if “reborn” is technical. Tertullian (c. 198 AD, On Baptism 18) opposes it, favoring delay for children. As Christianity grew, catechesis formalized baptism, reducing infant rites without preparation (Bakke, 2005).

Third Century

Infant baptism evidence emerges in the 3rd century (Aland, 2004, p. 10), though many parents delayed it until the 5th century for spiritual benefits, emergencies, or their own unbaptized status (Messmer, 2022, pp. 61-89; Jensen, 2012, p. 371). Tertullian, in On Baptism (c. 198 AD), opposed it despite its established use, favoring delay for personal faith (Ferguson, 1999, p. 58), a view out of step with North African practice where it thrived and possibly began (Strange, 1996, p. 123; Yılmaz, 2020, p. 375). Hippolytus (Apostolic Tradition 21.3-5) supported it, allowing parental proxy for young children (Ferguson, 1999, p. 58). Origen called it apostolic, its basis unclear (Ferguson, 1999, p. 58), while Cyprian urged immediate baptism for newborns, citing equal grace (Jensen, 2012, p. 371). A Rome inscription shows emergency baptisms occurred (Jensen, 2012, p. 371), though Aland (2004, p. 10) sees no new insight beyond texts.
