Beliefs

Nature of God

God desires a relationship with humanity, evident in His love (John 3:16) and self-revelation through Christ (Hebrews 1:3). Omnipotent and Sovereign: Creator and sustainer of all (Colossians 1:16; Genesis 1:1). Triune: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit coexist and work in unity (Matthew 28:19; John 15:26). Eternal and Immutable: Unchanging and everlasting (Psalm 90:2; Malachi 3:6). Holy and Just: Without darkness and against sin (1 John 1:5; Romans 1:18).

Nature of Jesus Christ

The Scriptures present Jesus Christ as the unique intersection of divinity and humanity. He is eternal, preexistent, and fully divine, participating in creation and sustaining all things (John 1:1-3, Colossians 1:16-17). At the same time, He took on human form to dwell among people, experiencing their struggles and limitations, yet without sin (John 1:14, Hebrews 4:15). Jesus serves as the Savior and Redeemer, bridging the gap between a holy God and fallen humanity (1 Timothy 2:5, John 3:16). He is Immanuel, God with us, demonstrating God’s love and justice while reigning as King and Judge (Isaiah 9:6, Hebrews 1:8). Thus, Jesus embodies the fullness of deity in bodily form and offers eternal life to all who believe in Him (Colossians 2:9, John 3:16).

Salvation

The overarching biblical narrative emphasizes that salvation is a gift of grace from God, obtained through faith in Jesus Christ and not through human works (Ephesians 2:8-9, Titus 3:5). Jesus Christ is the sole means of salvation, as affirmed in Acts 4:12 and John 14:6, which declare that no one can come to the Father except through Him. Salvation involves repentance, confession of faith, and a transformed life evidenced by alignment with God's will (Romans 10:9-10, Matthew 7:21).Salvation is both an act of divine mercy and an invitation to personal relationship and renewal, as seen in the work of the Holy Spirit in regeneration (Titus 3:5). It is universal in its offer (John 3:16-17), yet individual in its application, requiring belief and trust in Jesus (Romans 10:13, Acts 16:31). Moreover, while not earned by works, a life of obedience and good deeds reflects the faith that saves (Ephesians 2:10, James 2:18).Thus, salvation is a holistic process involving God’s initiative, human response, and the ongoing work of grace to transform lives, rooted in Christ’s atoning sacrifice and mediated through the Holy Spirit.

Authority of Scripture

Scripture, as conveyed in these passages, is divinely inspired and serves as the ultimate authority for teaching, correction, and guiding believers toward righteousness (2 Timothy 3:16-17). It is not a product of human invention but delivered through individuals carried by the Holy Spirit (2 Peter 1:20-21). Scripture holds enduring truth and is essential for understanding salvation, equipping believers for every good work, and revealing God's character and will (Psalm 119:160, John 5:39).Jesus affirmed the authority of Scripture, emphasizing its fulfillment in Him and its unchangeable nature (Matthew 5:17-18, John 10:35). The Word also serves as a foundation for discernment, wisdom, and spiritual growth, guided by the Holy Spirit, who enables understanding and application of its teachings (John 14:26, Hebrews 4:12).In essence, Scripture is the living and active Word of God, vital for faith and practice, providing both historical and spiritual insights necessary for a life aligned with God's purpose.

Sacraments

The sacramental acts of baptism and communion are consistently portrayed in Scripture as visible signs of inward grace, symbolizing and effecting a believer's union with Christ. Baptism represents repentance, spiritual cleansing, and incorporation into the body of Christ (Acts 2:38; 1 Peter 3:21; 1 Corinthians 12:13). Through baptism, individuals publicly declare their faith, align with Christ's death and resurrection, and receive the Holy Spirit (Romans 6:3-4; Matthew 3:16). Communion, instituted by Jesus at the Last Supper, serves as a memorial of His sacrifice and a means of spiritual nourishment for believers (1 Corinthians 11:23-26; John 6:51). By partaking in the bread and wine, participants proclaim Christ's death, affirm their covenant with God, and anticipate His return. The sacraments reflect the core principles of faith: grace, redemption, and the believer's active participation in the life of the Church. As outward acts of inward realities, they unify believers, emphasizing the communal and transformative nature of the Christian faith

Predestination vs. Free Will

Scripture reveals God's sovereignty in predestining salvation and humanity's responsibility to respond in faith. Predestination highlights God's control and purpose (Ephesians 1:11; Romans 8:29-30; 2 Timothy 1:9), while free will emphasizes human choice (Joshua 24:15; Deuteronomy 30:19). Verses like John 3:16 and Revelation 3:20 affirm salvation as an open invitation to all, showcasing a divine partnership where God offers grace, and humans respond in faith.

Afterlife

Scripture provides a clear portrayal of the afterlife, emphasizing both eternal reward and judgment. Those who follow Christ are promised eternal life in His presence (John 3:16; John 14:2), while those who reject God face eternal separation and punishment (Matthew 25:46; Revelation 21:8). The reality of heaven and hell is underpinned by God’s justice and grace, where believers, through faith, enter a place of peace and joy (Revelation 21:4) and the unrepentant experience consequences for their choices (Matthew 10:28). Eternal life is depicted as union with Christ, a transformative promise offered to all who believe (John 11:25-26). This hope is expressed in Christ’s assurance to the thief on the cross: “Today you will be with me in paradise” (Luke 23:43). The Bible also highlights judgment, with each person accountable for their deeds (Hebrews 9:27), emphasizing human responsibility to respond to God’s call (Deuteronomy 30:19).

Role of Mary

Mary, the mother of Jesus, is portrayed in Scripture as a model of humility, obedience, and faith. She accepts her role in God’s plan with the words, "Behold, I am the servant of the Lord; let it be to me according to your word" (Luke 1:38). As the one chosen to bear the Savior, she is greeted by the angel Gabriel as "favored one" and reassured of the Lord's presence (Luke 1:28, 30).Mary's unique role as the mother of Jesus is emphasized by Elizabeth's exclamation: "Blessed are you among women, and blessed is the fruit of your womb!" (Luke 1:42). Her Magnificat, a hymn of praise, reflects her deep understanding of God’s mercy and grace, recognizing her blessedness as part of God's redemptive plan (Luke 1:46-55). Mary is called "blessed" not only for her role but also for her faith in God's promises (Luke 1:45).Mary's influence extends beyond Jesus' birth. At the wedding at Cana, she intercedes with her Son, prompting His first miracle (John 2:1-5). At the crucifixion, Jesus entrusts Mary to the care of the beloved disciple, symbolically extending her role as a mother to all believers (John 19:26-27).While Mary is honored for her unique role in salvation history, Jesus emphasizes that true blessedness lies in hearing and obeying God’s word (Luke 11:27-28). This underscores Mary's significance not only as the mother of Jesus but also as a disciple who embodies faith and obedience.Through these narratives, Mary is presented as a humble servant of God, deeply faithful and integral to the story of salvation, while pointing all honor and glory to God.

Original Sin

Universal Human Depravity and Divine Redemption

Inherited Guilt (Federal Headship)Core Concept: Adam, as the representative (federal head) of humanity, bore responsibility for the initial disobedience that introduced sin into the world. His guilt is imputed to all humanity.Key Verses:Romans 5:12-19: "By one man's disobedience the many were made sinners."1 Corinthians 15:22: "As in Adam all die."Ezekiel 18:20: "The soul who sins shall die" (emphasizing personal responsibility alongside inherited guilt).2. Inherited Sinful Nature (Total Depravity)Core Concept: Humans inherit a corrupted nature from Adam, inclining them toward sin in every aspect of their being. This does not imply absolute depravity but an inability to achieve righteousness on their own.Key Verses:Psalm 51:5: "I was brought forth in iniquity."Jeremiah 17:9: "The heart is deceitful above all things."Romans 3:10-12: "None is righteous, no, not one."3. Personal Responsibility for SinCore Concept: Though humanity inherits a sinful nature and guilt, individuals are responsible for their personal sins and moral choices.Key Verses:Ezekiel 18:20: "The son shall not suffer for the iniquity of the father."James 4:17: "Whoever knows the right thing to do and fails to do it, for him it is sin."4. Separation from GodCore Concept: Sin causes a spiritual and relational separation between humanity and God, necessitating divine reconciliation.Key Verses:Isaiah 59:2: "Your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God."Romans 6:23: "The wages of sin is death."5. Universal Need for RedemptionCore Concept: Redemption is necessary because all are guilty before God and incapable of self-salvation.Key Verses:Romans 3:23: "For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."Romans 5:8: "While we were still sinners, Christ died for us."John 3:16: "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son."6. Victory and Transformation Through ChristCore Concept: Christ’s atoning work provides both justification from guilt and sanctification from sin's power, enabling believers to live in righteousness.Key Verses:Romans 8:1-2: "There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."2 Corinthians 5:21: "For our sake, he made him to be sin who knew no sin."Doctrine Espoused:This framework aligns with the doctrine of Original Sin in its dual emphasis on inherited guilt (federal headship) and the corrupted sinful nature (total depravity). Additionally, it incorporates aspects of personal responsibility and emphasizes the necessity of redemption through Christ, consistent with both Augustinian and Reformed theological traditions.

Baptism

"The Theology of Baptism: Covenant, Salvation, and Spirit"
1. Initiation into the Covenant CommunityDefinition: Baptism as an act of entering the body of Christ and the covenant community.Key Themes:Unity: Ephesians 4:5 - "One Lord, one faith, one baptism."Inclusion: 1 Corinthians 12:13 - "For in one Spirit we were all baptized into one body."Purpose: To signify membership in God’s covenant people, transcending ethnic, social, and cultural divisions.2. Symbol of Salvation and New LifeDefinition: Baptism as a representation of salvation, forgiveness, and spiritual rebirth.Key Themes:Forgiveness: Acts 2:38 - "Repent and be baptized...for the forgiveness of your sins."New Life: Romans 6:4 - "We were buried...by baptism into death...raised...to walk in newness of life."Purpose: To outwardly demonstrate the inner transformation brought by faith in Jesus Christ.3. Identification with Christ’s Death and ResurrectionDefinition: Baptism as a union with Christ in His death and resurrection.Key Themes:Death and Resurrection: Romans 6:3-4 - "Baptized into Christ Jesus...baptized into his death...raised...by the glory of the Father."Spiritual Renewal: Colossians 2:12 - "Buried with him in baptism, in which you were also raised with him."Purpose: To proclaim and participate in the redemptive work of Christ.4. Baptism of RepentanceDefinition: Baptism as an acknowledgment of sin and a commitment to repentance.Key Themes:John the Baptist’s Ministry: Mark 1:4 - "A baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins."Preparation: Luke 3:3 - "Proclaiming a baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins."Purpose: To prepare the heart for faith in Christ through repentance.5. Baptism in the Holy SpiritDefinition: Baptism as a spiritual act of being filled or empowered by the Holy Spirit.Key Themes:Empowerment: Acts 1:5 - "You will be baptized with the Holy Spirit not many days from now."Renewal: Titus 3:5 - "Washing of regeneration and renewal of the Holy Spirit."Purpose: To signify the believer’s reception of the Spirit for spiritual growth and empowerment.6. Mission and DiscipleshipDefinition: Baptism as an essential step in making disciples and fulfilling the Great Commission.Key Themes:Great Commission: Matthew 28:19 - "Go therefore and make disciples...baptizing them in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit."Evangelism: Mark 16:16 - "Whoever believes and is baptized will be saved."Purpose: To integrate new believers into the life of discipleship and the mission of the church.7. Symbol of Eschatological HopeDefinition: Baptism as a foretaste of the ultimate resurrection and life in the Kingdom of God.Key Themes:Eternal Life: John 3:5 - "Unless one is born of water and the Spirit, he cannot enter the kingdom of God."Resurrection: 1 Peter 3:21 - "Baptism...saves you...through the resurrection of Jesus Christ."Purpose: To anticipate and affirm the hope of eternal life in the presence of God.
