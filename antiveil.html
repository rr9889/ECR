Evidence 5

Stephen Bedale (1954)

Bedale's brief but influential article argued that the Greek word kephale (head) in Paul's writings was influenced by the Septuagint (LXX), where it translated the Hebrew ro'sh, which could mean "ruler" or "chief," but also "first" or "beginning" (Greek: arche).

Bedale suggested that in "biblical Greek," kephale and arche became approximate in meaning. For example, in Colossians 1:18, kephale as "ruler" or "chief" would be irrelevant; instead, it should be understood as "beginning" or "first principle" in the context of creation and redemption.

In passages like Colossians 2:19 and Ephesians 4:15, kephale as "overlord" makes little sense. Instead, understanding Christ as arche (the "beginning" or "source") clarifies how believers grow into Christ as the Second Adam.

Bedale acknowledged that kephale can sometimes mean "overlordship" (e.g., Ephesians 1:22), but in other contexts, it emphasizes priority or origin (arche), which carries the idea of authority (e.g., 1 Corinthians 11:3; Ephesians 5:23).

Bedale argued that while women are socially subordinate to men as part of the created order, they remain equal in spiritual status and capacities.

Bedale used the term "source" for kephale only in specific passages (Ephesians 4:15; Colossians 2:19), but his broader equivalence of kephale with arche extended the idea of "source" as "origin" or "first" to other contexts.

Bedale's views were adopted by several commentators, including Leon Morris (1958), C.K. Barrett (1968), and F.F. Bruce (1971).

A man praying with his head covered or a woman praying with her head uncovered dishonors their respective "heads." Despite differences in creation and dress, in Christ, "there is neither male nor female" (Galatians 3:28), emphasizing spiritual equality.    Many commentators have adopted Hooker's interpretation. Evidence 7

Judith M. Gundry-Volf argues that understanding Paul’s intent requires integrating creation, cultural-societal, and eschatological concerns, beyond the lexical debate.

Paul’s goal is to address behavioral problems that damage the credibility of the gospel in the wider society, particularly the shame/dishonor caused by improper head coverings in worship (vv. 4-6).

The issue is not women’s insubordination, homosexuality, or sexual provocation but rather the failure to respect cultural norms about gender, specifically the social practice of distinguishing male and female roles through head coverings.

The meaning of kephale (head) in verse 3 should be understood through verses 4-5, meaning honor or preeminence rather than authority or source. This acknowledges patriarchal connotations but emphasizes honor.

Verses 7-9 draw on patriarchal interpretations of Genesis 1 and 2, suggesting that a woman's head should be covered to show respect in a patriarchal society.

In verses 11-12, Paul introduces the concept of mutual dependence ("man comes through a woman") and that in the new creation, gender does not determine priority in the Lord, challenging hierarchical gender views.

Gundry-Volf identifies a tension between maintaining gender distinctions in the redeemed order while relativizing those distinctions socially, especially within a patriarchal context.

Evidence 9

David E. Garland interprets the head covering in 1 Corinthians 11 as a cultural symbol deeply tied to the honor-shame dynamics of 1st-century Corinthian society. He argues that the practice of wearing a head covering represented modesty, gender distinction, and social propriety, particularly for women. An uncovered head was a visible sign of rebellion or impropriety, which could bring shame to a woman's husband or father and disrupt the communal harmony of the church (pp. 224, 227). Drawing on the cultural context, Garland explains that the head covering functioned as a marker of a woman’s purity and respectability, ensuring she was perceived as a private person committed to guarding her honor (p. 227).

Garland emphasizes the double symbolism of the head covering, which both concealed and reflected a woman's dignity. He notes that the practice ensured appropriate decorum in worship, aligning with the cultural expectations of modesty and propriety in Corinth (p. 240). However, Garland is clear that Paul’s concern is not to enforce universal gender roles or hierarchical relationships but to uphold honor and respect in worship (p. 239). He argues that the head covering, within its cultural context, did not signify subordination but rather affirmed a woman’s authority and prerogative to pray and prophesy in public worship, a role she might not have previously enjoyed (p. 239).

Additionally, Garland explores the theological dimensions of Paul’s reasoning. For instance, he examines the connection between head coverings and angels in worship (v. 10), suggesting that the covering may symbolize dignity or respect in the presence of heavenly beings (pp. 241–242). While Garland finds this interpretation intriguing, he considers the evidence inconclusive. Ultimately, he highlights Paul’s larger goal: fostering a community where worship practices reflect cultural sensitivity without compromising the gospel.

Garland concludes that the specific practice of head coverings is culturally bound and not universally mandated. However, he affirms the underlying principle of maintaining honor and propriety in worship. In modern applications, Garland suggests that worship practices should be contextually appropriate, preserving unity and respect within the church community (p. 245).


Garland, D. E. (2003). 1 Corinthians. Baker Publishing Group. https://doi.org/10.5040/9781585583225

    Jill E. Marshall analyzes Paul’s discussion of head coverings in 1 Corinthians 11:2–16 as a response to specific cultural and rhetorical circumstances in Corinth. She notes that Paul’s argument begins with “cultural arguments” addressing the “practical, perhaps pressing, question: ‘What should the Corinthians wear on their heads while praying and prophesying?’” This reflects the “socio-cultural concept of shame” prevalent in the Greco-Roman world, where head coverings symbolized social norms of honor and propriety.

    Marshall highlights the flexibility of Paul’s reasoning, stating that “Paul does not strictly recite traditions as he does elsewhere but rather changes them by addition or reformulation.” By doing so, he adapts his argument to address the “rhetorical situation in Corinth” rather than presenting a universal or timeless rule. However, this approach results in what Marshall describes as an “ambiguous argument that does not provide a definitive, clear, or practical conclusion for his audience.”

    Paul’s use of “cultural arguments” is closely tied to the broader “quaestio about gender relationships in the community,” which Marshall identifies as the dominant issue in the passage. The cultural practice of head coverings serves as a symbol of these dynamics, and Paul reworks traditions to emphasize “gender distinction and interdependence rather than the dissolution of difference.” This suggests that the head coverings, like other traditions, were employed as “rhetorical proof” and shaped by the immediate concerns of the Corinthian context rather than being timeless or universal.

    Evidence 17


    Feminist Readings

    The debate over head coverings in 1 Corinthians 11 reflects broader tensions in Paul's writings about gender roles, cultural norms, and theological frameworks. Paul presupposes that women actively participate in rituals through prayer and prophecy (1 Cor. 11.2–16), yet the head covering emphasizes their subordinate status within a "hierarchy of heads." According to Zscharnack, the head covering symbolizes that “the woman has at best a secondary and deduced form of godlikeness,” serving as a visible marker of “her humbleness and condemned position” (as cited in Økland, 2004, p. 9). This reflects Paul’s alignment with patriarchal norms of the Greco-Roman context, where women’s modesty and submission were culturally emphasized.

    Feminist exegetes critique this perspective, arguing that it reinforces “a universe of sexist suppositions” (Daly, 1985, p. 5). Daly rejects the notion that passages like this can be dismissed as anomalies, instead pointing to their role in maintaining systemic patriarchy. Similarly, Wire interprets Paul’s instructions on head coverings as tied to male honor dynamics. She argues that Paul frames uncovered heads as detracting from God's glory, creating a "tension between self-glory associated with woman's uncovered head and God's glory associated with undistracted worship" (Wire, 1990, p. 122). In this way, the woman becomes “a factor in the problem that man has with God, or God with man” (Wire, 1990, p. 122).

    Historically, this tension has elicited varied interpretations. Zscharnack identifies two “seeds” in Paul’s texts: one reflecting equality, as in 1 Corinthians 11:11, and another rooted in the patriarchal values of antiquity. These seeds “cannot grow simultaneously,” yet Paul “evidently does not perceive the views as mutually exclusive since he mentions both of them in the same passage” (as cited in Økland, 2004, p. 9). Southworth, in The Woman’s Bible, dismisses the passage entirely, describing it as Paul adopting “an absurd old myth that angels got possession of women by laying hold of their hair” (Cady Stanton et al., 1898, p. 158).

    Some scholars, such as Eriksson (1998), contextualize Paul’s restrictions as addressing specific issues in Corinth. Paul’s emphasis on head coverings may be understood as his response to claims of esoteric revelations and the resulting “disorder in the worship services,” rather than a blanket condemnation of women’s participation in prayer and prophecy (p. 93). Wire’s rhetorical analysis supports this perspective, suggesting that Paul’s arguments reflect an attempt to control what he perceived as threats to his authority, especially from “the Corinthian women prophets,” who he believed contested his leadership (Wire, 1990, p. 8).

    The head covering debate, therefore, encapsulates the complexity of Pauline gender theology. While passages like 1 Corinthians 11 highlight patriarchal cultural norms, Paul simultaneously gestures toward mutual dependence and equality in Christ (1 Cor. 11:11). The tension between these elements continues to provoke scholarly debate about the interplay of culture, theology, and gender in early Christian communities.
