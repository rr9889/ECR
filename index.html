Celeberations

Christianity (before A.D. 325) was marked by its simplicity and focus on key events from the life of Jesus Christ and the practices of the early Church. The celebrations were primarily spiritual, deeply communal, and often linked to Jewish traditions that the early Christians inherited and adapted.

The writings sourcing 1st and 2nd-century Christian celebrations are drawn from The Didache (c. 100 A.D.), Tertullian's De Corona (c. 197 A.D.), Tertullian's Apologeticus (c. 197 A.D.), Hippolytus' Apostolic Tradition (c. 215 A.D.), Ignatius of Antioch's Letter to the Smyrnaeans (c. 110 A.D.), Irenaeus' Against Heresies (c. 180 A.D.), Pliny's Epistles (c. 112 A.D.), The Martyrdom of Polycarp (c. 156 A.D.), and the New Testament and Old Testament.
 

The Lord's Day

Every Sunday

The Lord's Day is explicitly identified as Sunday, the "first day of the week," established by early Christians to commemorate Jesus' resurrection. This practice emerged because Sunday was the day of Jesus’ resurrection (Acts 20:7, 1 Corinthians 16:2), and early writers like Ignatius of Antioch and Justin Martyr explained that it replaced the Jewish Sabbath (Saturday), symbolizing the new covenant and creation. On this day, Christians gathered for communal worship, which included reading scripture, prayers (eg. Lord's Prayer), and hymns. They also celebrated the Eucharist, sharing bread and wine to remember Christ’s sacrifice, made offerings to support the poor and their community, and fostered fellowship to strengthen unity, particularly during persecution. Sunday, often called the "eighth day," marked new creation and redemption, becoming central to Christian identity and joyfully replacing the Sabbath. It was observed in homes, catacombs, or secret locations due to persecution, as suggested by early accounts like Pliny’s Epistles (10.96), which describe Christians meeting in private before dawn to avoid detection.

Pascha

20 April 2025 and 5 April 2026

Pascha or Easter was the central celebration of Pre-Nicene Christianity, commemorating Jesus’ passion, death, and resurrection as the fulfillment of the Jewish Passover. All four Gospels affirm that Jesus' resurrection took place on the first day of the week, which is Sunday (Matthew 28:1-10, Mark 16:1-8, Luke 24:1-12, John 20:1-18).

The 1st and 2nd-century Christian consensus, as seen in Irenaeus (Against Heresies, Book 5) and Hippolytus (The Apostolic Tradition, c. 215 A.D.), holds that Easter (Pascha) is celebrated on the Sunday following the 14th of Nisan, the first Sunday after the full moon following the vernal equinox. Pope Anicetus of Rome supported this practice, aligning with the Roman tradition of observing Easter on Sunday rather than the 14th of Nisan (Irenaeus, Against Heresies, Book 3, Chapter 3).

This date makes it distinct from the Jewish Passover (Pesach), which occurs on the 14th of Nisan. Polycarp of Smyrna and Melito of Sardis, both bishops in Asia Minor, advocated for celebrating Easter on the 14th of Nisan, aligning it with the Jewish Passover.

Pascha (Easter) emphasized Christ’s victory over sin and death, symbolizing the new creation and hope for Christians (Irenaeus, Against Heresies, Book 5). Christians prepared for this celebration through fasting and catechesis, typically lasting 1-2 days (Hippolytus, The Apostolic Tradition, c. 215 A.D.). The occasion was marked by nighttime vigils, scripture readings, prayers, and a sunrise Eucharist, all reflecting the triumph of the resurrection (Tertullian, De Corona, Chapter 3). The event also symbolized renewal and hope, as believers celebrated Christ’s resurrection as the foundation of new life (Irenaeus, Against Heresies, Book 5). Communal meals and hymns highlighted unity and joy within the Christian community (The Didache, Chapters 9-10).

Pentecost

1 June 2025 and 21 May 2026

Pentecost, celebrated fifty days after Pascha, marked the descent of the Holy Spirit upon the apostles as described in Acts 2, where the Spirit enabled them to speak in various languages, leading to the conversion of about 3,000 people (Acts 2:1–4, 41). This event signified the beginning of the Church’s mission to spread the gospel universally and was seen as the fulfillment of Joel’s prophecy about God pouring out His Spirit on all people (Acts 2:16–21; Joel 2:28–32).

Pre-A.D. 325, Pentecost was observed as a day of worship and reflection, with Christians gathering for communal prayer, scripture readings, and breaking of bread (Acts 2:42). The Eucharist held a central place in these gatherings, symbolizing the unity of believers and their shared faith in Christ. Preaching likely focused on the role of the Holy Spirit in empowering the Church and spreading the gospel.

The Book of Acts suggests that baptisms were integral to Pentecost celebrations, as the Spirit's outpouring was closely associated with repentance and baptism (Acts 2:38–41). Fellowship and communal sharing were also emphasized, as believers shared meals and resources, strengthening their unity and commitment to one another (Acts 2:44–46).

Martyrs' Commemorations

Earthly death date of the martyr

Early Christians honored those who were killed for their faith, believing that martyrs entered eternal life on the day of their death, which was seen as their "heavenly birthday." Anniversaries of the martyrs’ deaths were observed annually, often coinciding with their execution dates. These commemorations celebrated the martyrs' unwavering faith, their sacrifice for Christ, and the hope of resurrection. Gatherings took place at the martyrs’ burial sites, such as in catacombs or marked graves, where Christians prayed, celebrated the Eucharist, and shared the stories of the martyrs to inspire courage and perseverance in their community. Stories like those of Polycarp of Smyrna and Ignatius of Antioch were read aloud. Commemorations sometimes included shared meals, reinforcing fellowship and remembrance of the martyrs’ example. These meals are linked to the Eucharist, focusing on Christ’s sacrifice and the martyrs' unity with Him.

“The blood of martyrs is the seed of the Church.” (Tertullian. Apologeticus. Translated c. 197. Chapter 50.)

"...The Lord shall grant us to celebrate the anniversary of his martyrdom, both in memory of those who have already finished their course, and for the exercising and preparation of those yet to walk in their steps." (Smyrnaeans. c. 156. The Martyrdom of Polycarp. Chapter 18.)

The Bible honors martyrs as faithful witnesses with a special place in God’s kingdom. Revelation 6:9–11 and 20:4 affirm their heavenly reward, while Jesus blesses the persecuted in Matthew 5:10–12 and urges courage in Matthew 10:28. Stephen’s martyrdom in Acts 7:54–60 models forgiveness and faith, forming a strong biblical basis for commemorating their sacrifice.

In the Bible, martyrs are honored as faithful witnesses with a special place in God’s kingdom, as affirmed in Revelation 6:9–11 and Revelation 20:4, while Matthew 5:10–12 and Matthew 10:28 bless those persecuted for righteousness and encourage courage in the face of death. Early Christian martyrs, including Stephen (stoned, Acts 7:54–60), James, Son of Zebedee (beheaded, Acts 12:1–2), and Antipas (martyred, Revelation 2:13), exemplify the sacrifices made for the faith. The apostles also faced martyrdom in various ways: Peter (crucified), Paul (beheaded), Andrew (crucified), Thomas (speared), Philip (crucified), Matthew (martyred), Bartholomew (flayed and beheaded), James, Son of Alphaeus (stoned or sawn in half), Thaddeus (martyred), Simon the Zealot (crucified), and Matthias (stoned and beheaded). Early Christian leaders like Polycarp of Smyrna (burned alive) and Ignatius of Antioch (eaten by wild beasts).

The early Christian community would have likely observed approxmitely 20-25 annual celebrations of martyrs.

Agape Feasts

No fixed date, a flexible practice, or tied to other Chrisitan events

The Agape Feast was a communal meal regularly celebrated by all members of the Christian community, including the poor and marginalized, often in conjunction with the Eucharist. These gatherings took place in homes or meeting spaces, where participants shared food and resources, reflecting Jesus’ teachings on generosity and care for others. The meals emphasized unity, charity, and Christ’s love, ensuring the needs of all were met and reinforcing equality within the Church. While initially linked to the Eucharist, abuses like those mentioned in 1 Corinthians 11:20–22 led to their separation over time. Through acts of mutual care, the Agape Feast fostered a deep sense of community and charity among early Christians.

The Agape Feasts are rooted in Jude 12, which directly references "love feasts" as communal meals among Christians. Acts 2:46 describes believers breaking bread in homes with joy and unity, while 1 Corinthians 11:20–22 critiques their misuse, calling for equality in such gatherings.

The Didache (Chapters 9–10) outlines shared meals connected to prayers and thanksgiving, and Tertullian (Apology 39) highlights them as acts of charity and fellowship for the poor. These sources establish the Agape Feasts as communal meals emphasizing unity, charity, and worship.

Baptism

No fixed date, could be standalone event, or tied to other Christian events

In the 1st and 2nd centuries, baptism was both a standalone event and, at times, part of a larger celebration, depending on the context.

Biblically, in the New Testament, baptism is closely linked to immediate conversion and repentance, with new converts often baptized shortly after accepting the faith (Acts 2:38, Acts 8:36-38, Acts 9:18, Acts 16:30-33). Baptism is presented as a standalone event associated with repentance, forgiveness, and the receiving of the Holy Spirit (e.g., Acts 2:38, Acts 8:36-38).

Early 1st-century Christian texts, such as the Didache, show that baptism often occurred alongside other rites like fasting and prayers but was also emphasized as a distinct event. Tertullian and Ignatius indicate that while baptism was a key act of Christian initiation, it was sometimes celebrated alongside the Eucharist but could also stand on its own (Tertullian, De Baptismo, Chapter 1; Ignatius of Antioch, Letter to the Ephesians, Chapter 20). Thus, while baptism was essential for Christian initiation, early Christian communities sometimes celebrated it in conjunction with other rites, such as the Eucharist, especially during larger gatherings or special occasions like Pentecost.

Lenten

Wednesdays and Fridays

Fasting in preparation for major feasts like Easter (Pascha) is mentioned in early Christian writings, particularly the Didache (c. 100 A.D.). The Didache (Chapters 8-9) provides clear instructions on fasting on Wednesdays and Fridays, which were seen as days of penance and preparation for Christian worship and communion. They were part of the early Christian discipline of preparing spiritually for the celebration of the Eucharist. The Didache emphasizes that fasting should be done in a manner that sets Christians apart from the practices of the Pharisees and hypocrites, suggesting a more spiritual and humble approach to fasting. This practice of fasting on Wednesdays and Fridays was widespread in early Christianity, with many Christian communities (Rome, Alexandria, and Asia Minor, Syria, North Africa) adopting it as a regular form of penance, spiritual reflection, and preparation for worship" (Didache, Chapters 8-9; Tertullian, De Corona, Chapter 3; Clement of Alexandria, Stromata, Book 3, Chapter 2).

Catechesis and Instruction

No fixed date, solely to be done pre-baptismal

In the Didache (c. 100 A.D.), "teaching" or "instruction" (didachē) refers to the moral guidance for new converts before baptism. In Hippolytus' Apostolic Tradition (c. 215 A.D.), the term "catechesis" (catechēseis) is used more formally for pre-baptismal instruction, focusing on Christian doctrine, confession, and ethical practices.

Early Christian catechesis was closely tied to baptism, serving as the foundational preparation for new converts. The Didache (c. 100 A.D.) provides basic instructions for baptizing new believers, including the essential elements of faith and moral conduct that candidates needed to demonstrate before baptism. It emphasizes the importance of teaching the commands of Jesus, and lays out a structure for pre-baptismal instruction, with particular focus on ethical living and adherence to Christian principles.

The Apostolic Tradition by Hippolytus (c. 215 A.D.) offers a more formalized and detailed approach to catechetical instruction, especially in the context of Christian initiation through baptism. Hippolytus outlines a thorough process where candidates undergo significant preparation for baptism, including confession of faith, instruction on Christian living, and moral teachings. His work also emphasizes the laying on of hands after baptism, marking the receipt of the Holy Spirit, further solidifying the initiation process. This catechesis was critical to ensuring that converts were fully grounded in the Christian faith and ready to join the community of believers through baptism.

Thus, in early Christianity, catechesis and baptism were inseparable, as baptism represented the culmination of one's spiritual preparation and entrance into the Christian community.

Ancillary Activities

No fixed date, as needed basis

Early Christians engaged in various ancillary activities within their communities that extended beyond formal worship and included teaching, study, fellowship, and acts of charity. These gatherings often took place in house churches, where believers came together for scripture readings, prayer, and Eucharistic celebrations. The Didache (c. 100 A.D.) outlines a broader communal lifestyle, encouraging activities like prayer, fasting, baptism, and teachings.

Scripture passages, such as Acts 2:42-47, describe early Christian communities as being devoted to the apostles' teaching, fellowship, breaking of bread, and prayer, suggesting that house-to-house gatherings were essential for both communal worship and spiritual education.

Paul’s letters further highlight the role of house churches in Christian teaching and fellowship. In Romans 16:5, he greets the church in Priscilla and Aquila's house, indicating that these gatherings were centers for study and community building. Similarly, 1 Corinthians 16:19 and Colossians 4:15 refer to house gatherings as important spaces for fellowship and teaching among early believers. These early house churches provided opportunities for Christian instruction, including Bible study, where believers could gather for teaching, mutual encouragement, and growth in faith.

Acts of charity were deeply rooted in both New Testament teachings and the practices of the 1st and 2nd-century church. Biblically, the Acts of the Apostles highlights the foundational Christian principle of shared resources for the welfare of the community. Acts 2:44-45 describes how early Christians sold their possessions to meet the needs of others, emphasizing that generosity and unity were core elements of Christian life (Acts 2:44-45). Similarly, in Acts 4:32-34, believers continued this practice of communal sharing, with figures like Barnabas donating land for the poor (Acts 4:32-34). Additionally, 1 Corinthians 16:1-2 and Galatians 2:10 further demonstrate that charitable collections were an integral, structured part of early Christian life, where Christians were called to set aside funds to support those in need, particularly the poor in Jerusalem (1 Corinthians 16:1-2, Galatians 2:10).

The Didache (c. 100 A.D.), a key early Christian text, reflects this principle by instructing Christians to give to the poor in accordance with God’s commands, advocating for generosity and secret charity (Didache, Chapter 1). Early writers like Tertullian (c. 197 A.D.) highlight how Christian charity was widely recognized, noting that Christians were known for their care for the poor, including orphans, widows, and prisoners (Tertullian, Apologeticus, Chapter 39). Ignatius of Antioch (c. 110 A.D.) also emphasized charity, urging the faithful to support church leaders and those persecuted for their faith (Ignatius of Antioch, Letter to the Smyrnaeans, Chapter 8).

In the 1st and 2nd centuries, acts of charity were not just social duties but expressions of Christian love and solidarity. Generosity towards the poor, support for church leaders, and assistance during persecution were foundational aspects of early Christian life. These acts were seen as practical manifestations of the gospel and covenantal living in a world where many believers faced economic hardships or persecution for their faith.
