Infant Baptism

First Century

A majority of scholars conclude that infant baptism was not widely practiced in the 1st century due to the absence of clear, explicit evidence (Ferguson, 2009; Wright, 2007; Cross & Livingstone, 2005; Grenz, 2000, p. 528). First-century Christians believed that small children, especially infants, were inherently sinless, so conversion was achieved by personally persuading children rather than through a formal baptismal rite for infants (Keeran, 2009, p. 11). The Epistle of Barnabas (c. 70 AD) similarly assumes infant sinlessness (Wiley, 2002, pp. 38–39). Thus, Christianity at this stage, had no specific doctrine of original sin (Obach, 2008, p. 41). Household baptisms are described in Acts and 1 Corinthians, Ferguson (2009, p. 178) contends that such descriptions are “hardly applicable to small children” and that inferring infant baptism from these texts is an “argument from silence.”

Second Century

Apologists such as Aristdes and Athengoras affirm small children who die early are sinless indeed.
