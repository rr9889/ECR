Infant Baptism in Early Christianity

1st Century

The majority consensus among scholars is that infant baptism likely became a widespread practice by the 2nd century, but there is no clear evidence that it was practiced in the 1st century. The biblical texts provide strong implications but not explicit, clear evidence that infant baptism was practiced in the 1st century.

They point to Bible passages where entire households were baptized, suggesting that young children might have been part of these baptisms. Key biblical passages often cited to support infant baptism include:

Matthew 19:14 - "But Jesus said, ‘Allow the children, and do not forbid them to come to Me, for of such is the Kingdom of the Heavens.’"

Acts 2:38-39 - "And Peter said to them, ‘Repent, and be baptized each of you on the Name of Jesus Christ for forgiveness of sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit, for the promise is to you and to your children, and to all those afar off, as many as the Lord our God will call.’"

Acts 16:15 - "And when she was baptized, and her household, she called on us, saying, ‘If you have judged me to be faithful to the Lord, having come into my house, remain’; and she constrained us."

Acts 16:33 - "And having taken them in that hour of the night, he washed from their blows, and was baptized—himself and all his household—immediately."

1 Corinthians 1:16 - "Yes, I also baptized the household of Stephanas; besides, I do not know if I baptized any other."

Based on the biblical texts, while there is no explicit mention of infant baptism, the inclusion of τέκνα (tekna, "children") in Acts 2:38-39 and οἶκος (oikos, "household") in Acts 16:15, Acts 16:33, and 1 Corinthians 1:16 strongly implies that infants and young children were likely baptized alongside adults. The term οἶκος in the ancient context encompassed the entire family unit—parents, children, servants, and sometimes extended family—suggesting baptism was inclusive of all age groups. Thus, it is reasonable to conclude that infants and children were part of early Christian baptismal practices.

Iconography and catacombs

The evidence for infant baptism in early Christian iconography and catacombs is indirect but becomes clearer over time. In the 2nd century, some epitaphs in Roman catacombs, such as the Epitaph of Eutychius (late 2nd or early 3rd century), mention children who were baptized before death, suggesting the practice may have existed by this time. By the 3rd century, small baptismal fonts, like those in Dura-Europos, and additional epitaphs provide stronger evidence. Baptismal scenes in catacombs, such as the Catacomb of Callixtus, also feature small figures that may represent children. While there are no explicit depictions of infants being baptized, these clues indicate that infant baptism was likely practiced by the 3rd century.

Early Church Fathers

Early Church Fathers almost consistently affirmed infant baptism as a practice rooted in apostolic tradition. Below are key quotations from prominent figures:

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 130–202 AD)


"He came to save all through Himself—infants, children, youths, and old men." (Against Heresies, Book II, 22.4)

Tertullian (c. 160–220 AD)

"The delay of baptism is more profitable, according to everyone’s condition and disposition, and even age: and especially in the case of little children." (On Baptism, Chapter 18)

Note: Tertullian advised delaying baptism, particularly for infants, due to concerns about post-baptismal sin and the need for personal commitment to the faith. His view was a minority position in the early Church.

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170–235 AD)

"Baptize first the children, and if they can speak for themselves, let them do so. Otherwise, let their parents or other relatives speak for them." (Apostolic Tradition, 21)

Origen (c. 184–253 AD)

"The Church received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism even to infants." (Commentary on Romans, Book V, 9)

Cyprian of Carthage (c. 200–258 AD)

"If remission of sins is granted even to the worst sinners, how much more should an infant not be held back, who has done no sin." (Letters, 64:2)


Baptism Requirements

Full Article on Baptism
  1. Repentance and Faith: Baptism is tied to repentance and faith (Acts 2:38). For infants, proxy faith (parents/Church) fulfills this requirement.
  2. Trinitarian Formula: Baptism is performed in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit (Matthew 28:19).
  3. Use of Water: Baptism requires water, preferably "living water" (running water), though other sources are acceptable (Didache 7:1-2).
  4. Fasting: Both the baptizer and candidate fast before baptism (Didache 7:4). For infants, sponsors or parents fast on their behalf.
  5. Undressing: Symbolizes shedding the "old self" and rising to new life in Christ (Colossians 3:9-10). Nakedness mirrors Christ’s death and resurrection (Romans 6:3-4). Tertullian (On Baptism, Chapter 20) and Hippolytus of Rome (Apostolic Tradition) describe this practice, with modesty maintained through separate baptisms for men and women.
  6. Ages: Infants are included as part of the covenant community, reflecting the belief that baptism is for all believers, regardless of age. Origen (Commentary on Romans, Book V, 9) and Cyprian of Carthage (Letters, 64:2) affirm infant baptism as an apostolic tradition, noting its necessity for the remission of original sin.
  7. Symbolism: Baptism symbolizes dying to sin and rising to new life in Christ (Romans 6:4), essential for spiritual rebirth (John 3:5).

Infants Requirements

The question of how infants can meet the requirements of repentance, faith, and fasting—despite their inability to consciously fulfill these actions—was addressed by the Early Church Fathers. Their explanations provide an understanding on how and the why.

Infants and Repentance

The Church Fathers believed that infants are born with original sin inherited from Adam (Psalm 51:5, Romans 5:12). Baptism was seen as the means to cleanse them from this inherited sin, even though they could not consciously repent.

Church Father's View:

  • Cyprian of Carthage (c. 200–258 AD) argued that infants should not be denied baptism simply because they cannot repent, as they are still affected by sin:

    "If remission of sins is granted even to the worst sinners, how much more should an infant not be held back, who has done no sin." (Letters, 64:2)

Infants and Faith

The Fathers taught that infants are part of the covenant community and can receive the grace of baptism through the faith of their parents or the Church.

  • Origen (c. 184–253 AD) affirmed that infant baptism was an apostolic tradition, and the faith of the Church or parents stood in place of the infant's personal faith:

    "The Church received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism even to infants." (Commentary on Romans, Book V, 9)

  • Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170–235 AD) instructed that if infants could not speak for themselves, their parents or relatives should speak on their behalf during baptism:

    "Baptize first the children, and if they can speak for themselves, let them do so. Otherwise, let their parents or other relatives speak for them." (Apostolic Tradition, 21)

Infants and Fasting

Fasting before baptism was a discipline for those preparing for the sacrament. Infants, however, were exempt from this requirement due to their age and inability to fast.

The Didache (late 1st or early 2nd century) calls for fasting for the baptizer and the candidate, but it does not explicitly mention infants. The practice of infant baptism likely did not require fasting for the child, as it was understood that infants could not participate in this discipline.
  • The focus was instead on the sponsors (godparents) or parents, who would fast on behalf of the child as part of the preparation for baptism.

Infants and the Covenant Community

The Church Fathers viewed baptism as the New Covenant equivalent of circumcision (Colossians 2:11-12). Just as infants were included in the Old Covenant through circumcision, they were included in the New Covenant through baptism.

Church Father's View:

  • Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 130–202 AD) emphasized that Christ came to save all, including infants:

    "He came to save all through Himself—infants, children, youths, and old men." (Against Heresies, Book II, 22.4)

The Role of Sponsors (Godparents)

Since infants cannot personally fulfill the requirements of repentance, faith, or fasting, the Church Fathers emphasized the role of sponsors (godparents) or parents to act on their behalf.

Church Father's View:

  • Tertullian (c. 160–220 AD), while advocating for delayed baptism, acknowledged that sponsors could speak for infants during the baptismal rite.
  • Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170–235 AD) explicitly instructed that parents or relatives should speak for infants during baptism if the child could not speak for themselves.

Conclusion

Is it necessary?

While the 1st century does not provide explicit evidence of infant baptism, the 2nd and 3rd centuries show a clear development of the practice, supported by both historical evidence and the theological arguments of the Early Church Fathers. Thus, infant baptism was viewed as a necessary and integral part of early Christian practice.

Overall conclusion

The biblical texts and historical developments strongly suggest its inclusion by the 2nd and 3rd centuries. Key biblical passages, such as those mentioning household baptisms (e.g., Acts 16:15, 33), imply that infants and children were likely baptized alongside adults. The Early Church Fathers, including figures like Irenaeus of Lyons, Origen, and Cyprian of Carthage, consistently affirmed infant baptism as a practice rooted in apostolic tradition, emphasizing its necessity for the forgiveness of original sin and inclusion in the covenant community. Theological justifications provided by the Church Fathers addressed the challenges of infants not being able to consciously repent, express faith, or fast.

They argued that Original Sin necessitated baptism for infants and the faith of the Church or parents could stand in place of the infant's personal faith. Also that Baptism was the New Covenant equivalent of circumcision, which included infants in the Old Covenant. By the 3rd century, historical evidence, such as small baptismal fonts and catacomb inscriptions, further supports the practice of infant baptism. In summary, infant baptism was considered necessary by the Early Church Fathers for the salvation and inclusion of infants in the Christian community.
