Infant Baptism

1st Century

The majority consensus among scholars is that infant baptism was not practiced in the 1st century due to lack of clear evidence. Infant baptism likely became a widespread practice by the 2nd century or 3rd century.REF Scholarly Consensus on Infant Baptism
- Ferguson, E. (2009). Baptism in the Early Church: History, Theology, and Liturgy in the First Five Centuries. Grand Rapids: Eerdmans.
- Wright, D. F. (2007). Infant Baptism in Historical Perspective. Paternoster Press.
- Jeremias, J. (1960). The Origins of Infant Baptism. London: SCM Press.
- Cross, F. L., & Livingstone, E. A. (Eds.). (2005). The Oxford Dictionary of the Christian Church. Oxford University Press.

The biblical texts provide unclear implications that infant baptism was practiced in the 1st century. Proponents of this view, point to Bible passages where entire households were baptized, suggesting that young children might have been part of these baptisms. Key biblical passages often cited to support infant baptism include:

Matthew 19:14 - "But Jesus said, ‘Allow the children, and do not forbid them to come to Me, for of such is the Kingdom of the Heavens.’"

Problem with this verse: The account of Jesus blessing the little children (Matthew 19:13-15; Mark 10:13-16; Luke 18:15-17) has occasionally been used to support the practice of infant baptism. However, none of the Synoptic Gospels place this event in a baptismal context. Additionally, the phrase in Matthew 18:3, "to become as children," indicates that those being addressed were not children themselves, making it clear that this passage does not serve as a reference to infant baptism (Ferguson, 2009, p. 138).REF Ferguson, E. (2009). Baptism in the early church: History, theology, and liturgy in the first five centuries. Wm. B. Eerdmans Publishing.

Acts 2:38-39 - "And Peter said to them, ‘Repent, and be baptized each of you on the Name of Jesus Christ for forgiveness of sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit, for the promise is to you and to your children, and to all those afar off, as many as the Lord our God will call.’"

Acts 16:15 - "And when she was baptized, and her household, she called on us, saying, ‘If you have judged me to be faithful to the Lord, having come into my house, remain’; and she constrained us."

Acts 16:33 - "And having taken them in that hour of the night, he washed from their blows, and was baptized—himself and all his household—immediately."

1 Corinthians 1:16 - "Yes, I also baptized the household of Stephanas; besides, I do not know if I baptized any other."

Problem with these verses: Acts and 1 Corinthians mention household baptisms, such as the baptism of Cornelius’ household (Acts 10). However, as Ferguson notes, these descriptions "are hardly applicable to small children" (p. 178). Ferguson also notes that there is no direct evidence of infant baptism in the 1st century. Arguments for its practice during this time are based on inference and are, as he describes, "an argument from silence" logical fallacy (p. 178).REF Ferguson, E. (2009). Baptism in the early church: History, theology, and liturgy in the first five centuries. Wm. B. Eerdmans Publishing.

Furthermore, the phrase "for you and your children" is part of a broader promise that includes "all who are far off" (likely referring to future generations or Gentiles). While τέκνα can refer to children of any age, the context does not explicitly specify infants or young children. Ferguson, E. (2009). Baptism in the early church: History, theology, and liturgy in the first five centuries. Wm. B. Eerdmans Publishing. p. 178.

Iconography and catacombs

The evidence for infant baptism in early Christian iconography and catacombs is indirect but becomes clearer over time. In the 2nd century, some epitaphs in Roman catacombs, such as the Epitaph of Eutychius (late 2nd or early 3rd century), mention children who were baptized before death, suggesting the practice may have existed by this time. By the 3rd century, small baptismal fonts, like those in Dura-Europos, and additional epitaphs provide stronger evidence. Baptismal scenes in catacombs, such as the Catacomb of Callixtus, also feature small figures that may represent children. While there are no explicit depictions of infants being baptized, these clues indicate that infant baptism was likely practiced by the 3rd century.

Early Church Fathers

Early Church Fathers almost consistently affirmed infant baptism as a practice rooted in apostolic tradition. Below are key quotations from prominent figures:

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 130–202 AD)


"He came to save all through Himself—infants, children, youths, and old men." (Against Heresies, Book II, 22.4)

Tertullian (c. 160–220 AD)

"The delay of baptism is more profitable, according to everyone’s condition and disposition, and even age: and especially in the case of little children." (On Baptism, Chapter 18)

Note: Tertullian advised delaying baptism, particularly for infants, due to concerns about post-baptismal sin and the need for personal commitment to the faith. His view was a minority position in the early Church.

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170–235 AD)

"Baptize first the children, and if they can speak for themselves, let them do so. Otherwise, let their parents or other relatives speak for them." (Apostolic Tradition, 21)

Origen (c. 184–253 AD)

"The Church received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism even to infants." (Commentary on Romans, Book V, 9)



Infants Requirements?

The Early Church Fathers addressed how infants, unable to consciously repent, express faith, or fast, could meet baptismal requirements. They argued that infants inherit original sin (Psalm 51:5, Romans 5:12) and require baptism for its remission, even without personal repentance. Cyprian of Carthage (c. 200–258 AD) asserted that infants, though sinless in action, should not be denied baptism: "If remission of sins is granted even to the worst sinners, how much more should an infant not be held back, who has done no sin" (*Letters*, 64:2).

Regarding faith, the Fathers taught that infants could receive baptism through the faith of their parents or the Church. Origen (c. 184–253 AD) stated that infant baptism was an apostolic tradition: "The Church received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism even to infants" (*Commentary on Romans*, Book V, 9). Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170–235 AD) instructed that parents or relatives should speak for infants during baptism if the child could not (*Apostolic Tradition*, 21).

Fasting before baptism, required for older candidates, was not expected of infants. Instead, sponsors or parents fasted on their behalf. The *Didache* (late 1st or early 2nd century) mentions fasting for baptismal candidates but does not specify infants, implying their exemption.

The Fathers also linked baptism to circumcision (Colossians 2:11-12), viewing it as the New Covenant inclusion of infants. Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 130–202 AD) emphasized Christ’s salvation for all, including infants: "He came to save all through Himself—infants, children, youths, and old men" (*Against Heresies*, Book II, 22.4).

Sponsors (godparents) played a key role, acting on behalf of infants who could not fulfill baptismal requirements themselves. Tertullian (c. 160–220 AD) and Hippolytus both acknowledged this practice, with Hippolytus explicitly instructing parents or relatives to speak for infants during baptism.


Dissenting Opinion

On Baptism (c. 198)

Quintus Septimius Florens Tertullianus

Chapter 18. Of the Persons to Whom, and the Time When, Baptism is to Be Administered

"Therefore, considering each person’s situation, character, and even age, it is better to delay baptism—especially in the case of young children. Why rush to baptize them if baptism itself is not an urgent necessity? Doing so also places the sponsors (godparents) at risk, as they may fail to keep their promises due to death or be let down if the child grows up to live a life contrary to the faith. After all, the Lord said, 'Do not hinder them from coming to me.' So let them come to Christ as they grow older, as they learn, and as they come to understand what it means to follow Him. Let them become Christians when they are capable of knowing Christ for themselves. Why should the innocence of childhood be hurried into seeking the 'remission of sins'? We are more cautious in worldly matters—why would we entrust divine things to those we wouldn’t even trust with earthly responsibilities? Let them first learn to seek salvation for themselves, so that when they ask, it can truly be said that you have given 'to the one who asks.'"

Infant Mortality Rate

The expansion of the Christian community in the 2nd and 3rd centuries, along with the inclusion of families, significantly influenced the normalization of infant baptism. High child mortality rates further motivated parents to seek baptism for their children's spiritual security. Everett Ferguson argues that while there is no firm evidence for infant baptism before the late second century, its origins likely lie in the emergency baptism of sick children expected to die soon. REF Ferguson, E. (2009). Baptism in the Early Church: History, Theology, and Liturgy in the First Five Centuries. Wm. B. Eerdmans Publishing.

In A Woman’s Place: House Churches in Early Christianity, Osiek and MacDonald highlight the high risks of infection during childbirth, as noted in Pliny the Elder’s Natural History. They also report that approximately 30% of infants died in their first year, reflecting the high infant mortality rates of the time. REF Osiek, C., & MacDonald, M. (2006). A Woman's Place: House Churches in Earliest Christianity. Fortress Press.

Historical evidence supports this perspective, notably Cyprian's advocacy for baptizing infants without delay and the practice of emergency baptisms for gravely ill children. This practice reflected the early Christian community's concern for ensuring the salvation of vulnerable infants. REF Cyprian of Carthage, Epistle 64 (c. 250 CE).

David F. Wright builds on this, suggesting that infant baptism became more widespread following Augustine's theological emphasis on the necessity of baptism for salvation, particularly in light of original sin. REF Wright, D. F. (2007). What Has Infant Baptism Done to Baptism? Paternoster Press.

Finally, Francesco Arduini connects the practice to historical crises like the Antonine Plague, which heightened fears of child mortality and accelerated the adoption of infant baptism as a protective rite. REF Arduini, F. (2020). "The Pandemic Roots of Infant Baptism." Biblical Archaeology Society.

Conclusion

While the 1st century lacks explicit evidence of infant baptism, the practice became well-established by the 2nd and 3rd centuries, supported by historical records and theological arguments from Early Church Fathers. Biblical passages referencing household baptisms (e.g., Acts 16:15, 33) suggest infants and children were likely baptized with adults. Figures like Irenaeus, Origen, and Cyprian affirmed infant baptism as apostolic tradition, emphasizing its role in forgiving original sin and integrating infants into the covenant community. Theologically, the Church Fathers addressed concerns about infants' inability to repent or express faith by arguing that the faith of the Church or parents could substitute for the child’s personal faith. They also likened baptism to circumcision in the Old Covenant, which included infants. By the 3rd century, archaeological evidence, such as small baptismal fonts and catacomb inscriptions, further confirms the practice. In summary, infant baptism was viewed by the Early Church as essential for the salvation and inclusion of infants in the Christian community.
