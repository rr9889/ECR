Infant Baptism

Early Christian worship was far from uniform. Rather than a single “apostolic” tradition, liturgical practices before the 325 AD varied significantly by culture, language, and theological outlook. Worship in the first three centuries encompassed diverse rites, such as initiation, across Armenian, Syrian, Greek, Coptic, and Latin communities (Johnson, 2018).

Conclusion: There is no Apostolic, Historical or Biblical tradition prescribed to infants getting baptized.

1st Century

A majority of scholars conclude that infant baptism was not widely practiced in the 1st century due to the absence of clear, explicit evidence (Ferguson, 2009; Jeremias, 1960; Wright, 2007; Cross & Livingstone, 2005; Grenz, 2000, p. 528).

First-century Christians believed that small children, especially infants, were inherently sinless, so conversion was achieved by personally persuading children rather than through a formal baptismal rite for infants (Keeran, 2009, p. 11). This view is supported by early texts; for example, the Didache (c. 80 AD) favors adult baptism, implying that its author held that children were born sinless, and the Epistle of Barnabas (c. 70 AD) similarly assumes infant sinlessness (Wiley, 2002, pp. 38–39). Thus, Christianity at this stage, had no specific doctrine of original sin (

Moreover, Matthew 18:3’s phrase “to become as children” clearly addresses mature believers rather than young children (Ferguson, 2009, p. 138).

The biblical texts provide unclear implications regarding the practice of infant baptism in the 1st century. Proponents argue that passages referring to the baptism of entire households—such as Acts 2:38–39; Acts 16:15, 16:33; and 1 Corinthians 1:16—suggest that infants may have been included. 2nd Century


Clement of Rome’s “1 Clement” and the letters of Ignatius of Antioch (Letter to the Smyrnaeans), which assume universal sin without discussing its origin. In contrast, Clement of Alexandria’s works, such as “The Instructor” hint at the idea that sin is inherited from Adam, though without much elaboration (Wiley, 2002, pp. 38–39).

Historical studies (Ferguson, 2009; Wall, 2003) show that ambiguous 2nd-century evidence, often seen as emergency baptisms, does not indicate a universal practice of infant baptism. Instead, factors such as community expansion, family inclusion, and high infant mortality led to its eventual normalization. Ferguson (2009) argues that infant baptism likely began as an emergency measure for critically ill children, while Osiek and MacDonald (2006) highlight that risks during childbirth and a 30% infant mortality rate drove parents to secure their children’s spiritual safety, as reflected in Cyprian’s advocacy for prompt baptism.

Some early Christian burial epitaphs suggest that, in cases of severe illness when parents feared their child might die, baptism was sometimes administered (Ferguson, Encyclopedia of Early Christianity, 162). In contrast, other epitaphs make no mention of infant baptism (Keeran, 2009, p.11).
Regarding Irenaeus of Lyons’ statement to "[baptize] infants, children, youths, and old men" (Against Heresies, Book II, 22.4), dated around 174 AD, a careful reading of the full context reveals that his language is symbolic. Irenaeus uses the imagery of Christ “becoming an infant for infants” to illustrate that Christ sanctifies every stage of life. Rather than issuing a direct, doctrinal command that infants must be baptized, the text employs metaphor to show how Christ identifies with all ages.
In Chapter 18 of On Baptism (written around 198 AD), Tertullian directly addresses the doctrine of infant baptism, asserting that “The delay of baptism is more profitable, according to everyone’s condition and disposition, and even age: and especially in the case of little children.”

3rd Century and later

Small baptismal fonts at Dura-Europos and epitaphs referencing baptism, has been interpreted by some scholars as indicative of infant baptism. Additionally, the depiction of small figures in baptismal scenes in catacombs, like those in the Catacomb of Callixtus, is argued to represent children. However, critics note that the diminutive scale of fonts and figures might reflect stylistic choices or practical constraints rather than a direct reference to the baptism of infants.

In 215 AD, Hippolytus of Rome developed the Rome doctrine of infant baptism rituals (Keeran, 2009). Hewrote in the Apostolic Tradition (Chapter 21): “Baptize first the children, and if they can speak for themselves, let them do so. Otherwise, let their parents or other relatives speak for them.”

Origen, writing around 246 AD, stated in his Commentary on Romans (Book V, 9) that “the Church received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism even to infants.”

Augustine argued that baptism was essential for salvation, contending that infant baptism demonstrated that even infants were affected by original sin and held accountable for it (On Marriage and Concupiscence 2.29.51, as cited in Pelikan, 2009, p. 292). He also noted that the exorcisms performed during baptism indicated that children were vulnerable to demonic influence (On the Merits and Remission of Sins 1.23, 28, 39; 3.2.7; 3.3.6, as cited in Pelikan, 2009, p. 292; Ferguson, 2009, p. 162). This theological emphasis helped cement the practice of infant baptism in the broader church.

Infant baptism helped refine the doctrine of original sin, a connection first clearly articulated by Cyprian, who argued that invoking original sin validated a practice whose apostolic credentials were unquestioned (Pelikan, 2009, pp. 165, 291, 292). Cyprian also reports that an African council ruled that newborns should not be barred from baptism, and by the fifth century, infant baptism was increasingly justified as a means of forgiving original sin, leading to its wider adoption (Lane, 2004, p. 117; Keeran, 2009, p. 12).
