Celebrations

Glorying The Lord Christ Jesus

Agape Feasts

The Agape Feast was an early Christian communal meal rooted in charity, unity, and worship, often held in homes and open to all, including the poor. While initially tied to the Eucharist, it had no fixed date and was shaped by flexible, local practices. Scriptural and early Christian sources—such as Jude 12, Acts 2:46, 1 Corinthians 11:20–22, the Didache, and Tertullian—affirm its focus on fellowship, mutual care, and equality within the Church. Over time, due to abuses, the Agape Feast became distinct from the Eucharist.

Pascha

Pascha (Easter) was the central feast of Pre-Nicene Christianity, celebrating Jesus’ death and resurrection as the fulfillment of Passover. Early Christians, including Irenaeus and Hippolytus, agreed it should be observed on the Sunday after the 14th of Nisan—the first Sunday after the full moon following the spring equinox. While Rome supported this Sunday observance, figures like Polycarp and Melito of Sardis favored celebrating on the 14th itself. The feast symbolized Christ’s victory over death and the promise of new life, marked by fasting, catechesis, vigils, scripture, prayer, Eucharist at dawn, communal meals, and hymns of joy.

Lord's Day

Every Sunday, Christians observed the Lord’s Day to commemorate Jesus’ resurrection (Acts 20:7; 1 Cor. 16:2). Replacing the Jewish Sabbath, Sunday was called the "first" and "eighth" day, symbolizing new creation and covenant (Ignatius, Magnesians 9; Justin Martyr; Epistle of Barnabas). Gatherings featured scripture, prayer, hymns, Eucharist, and offerings for the poor (Didache; Justin Martyr). Amid Roman persecution, worship often occurred secretly in homes or catacombs (Pliny, Epistles 10.96), reinforcing Christian identity through shared faith and solidarity (Acts 2:44–45; Tertullian).

Ancillary Activities

Ancillary Activities, held as needed without a fixed date, reflected early Christians’ daily commitment to faith through teaching, fellowship, and charity. House churches served as hubs for scripture, prayer, Eucharist, and communal meals (Didache; Acts 2:42–47; Rom. 16:5). Believers shared resources, supported the poor, and emphasized generosity as central to Christian life (Acts 2:44–45; 1 Cor. 16:1–2; Tertullian; Ignatius), embodying love, unity, and care for the vulnerable.

Catechesis and Instruction

Catechesis and Instruction, conducted solely before baptism and without a fixed date, were essential for preparing converts in early Christianity. The Didache (c. 100 A.D.) offered basic moral teachings and baptismal guidelines, stressing ethical conduct and Jesus’ commands. By c. 215 A.D., Hippolytus' Apostolic Tradition formalized this into a structured process of catechesis—covering doctrine, confession, and Christian living. This instruction ensured converts were spiritually grounded and ready to enter the Church through baptism and receive the Holy Spirit.

Fasting

Fasting was regularly observed on Wednesdays and Fridays in early Christianity as a form of penance and spiritual preparation. The Didache (c. 100 A.D.) instructed believers to fast on these days to distinguish themselves from non-Christian practices and to ready themselves for worship and the Eucharist. This discipline, rooted in humility and reflection, became widespread across Christian communities in Rome, Alexandria, Asia Minor, Syria, and North Africa (Didache 8–9; Tertullian, De Corona 3; Clement, Stromata 3.2).

The Day of Atonement

Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16), was the most solemn day in ancient Israel—a day of fasting, repentance, and sacrifice. In 2025, Yom Kippur begins at sundown on October 1 and ends at nightfall on October 2. In 2026, it begins the evening of September 20 and ends the evening of September 21.  This holy day foreshadowed the ultimate work of Jesus Christ, our Great High Priest (Hebrews 4:14), who offered Himself as the perfect, once-for-all sacrifice for sin (Hebrews 9:11–14).  In biblical tradition, the High Priest would enter the Holy of Holies only on this day, sprinkling blood on the kapporet (atonement cover) to symbolically cleanse Israel from sin. A second goat—the scapegoat—was sent into the wilderness bearing the people's sins (Leviticus 16:21–22), picturing Christ "bearing our sins in His body" (1 Peter 2:24).  The blood sacrifice, a central theme of Yom Kippur, points directly to Christ’s death on the cross as the true atonement: “Without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness” (Hebrews 9:22; Leviticus 17:11). In Him, believers are cleansed, reconciled to God, and permanently written in the Lamb’s Book of Life (Revelation 21:27).  While traditional observance includes fasting, confession, and prayer, Christians can view Yom Kippur as a time to reflect with gratitude on the depth of God’s mercy through Jesus. His sacrifice removes sin completely—not just covers it—and grants eternal access to God (Hebrews 10:19–22).

Pentecost

Pentecost, celebrated 50 days after Easter, falls on June 1, 2025, and May 24, 2026. It marks the descent of the Holy Spirit on the disciples (Acts 2), fulfilling Jesus’ promise (John 15:26) and empowering the Church for its mission. It signifies the birth of the Church and God’s ongoing presence in the world through the Holy Spirit. Symbols of Pentecost include fire, wind, and a dove, representing God's power, breath of life, and peace. Bishops wear mitres shaped like flames, echoing the “tongues of fire” on the apostles’ heads. Christians celebrate Pentecost to remember that God is active in and through us, equipping us with comfort, guidance, and strength to carry out His mission.

Days of Unleavened Bread

The Days of Unleavened Bread, a seven-day festival beginning on the 15th of Nisan (Leviticus 23:6), were observed by Jesus, His disciples, and the early Church—even among Gentile believers (see Matthew 26:17; Acts 20:6; 1 Corinthians 5:7–8).  During this time, leaven (yeast) is removed from the diet and home, symbolizing the removal of sin, malice, and hypocrisy. Paul encourages believers to “keep the feast… with the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth” (1 Corinthians 5:8), affirming its ongoing spiritual significance.  Christians may view this feast as a powerful opportunity to renew their hearts, reject sin, and walk in authenticity and holiness, remembering that Christ, our Passover, was sacrificed for us (1 Corinthians 5:7).  In 2025, the Days of Unleavened Bread will be observed from April 13–19; in 2026, from April 2–8.

Passover

In the 1st and 2nd centuries, followers of Jesus commemorated the Last Supper within the context of the Jewish Passover, which Jesus Himself observed with His disciples. This meal, likely held on the evening of the 14th of Nisan, included the breaking of bread and sharing of wine, which Jesus reinterpreted as symbols of His body and blood (Luke 22:19–20; 1 Corinthians 11:23–26). During the meal, Jesus washed His disciples’ feet (John 13:5–14), a profound act of servant leadership that became a model for early Christian humility and mutual care.  Early Christians in Asia Minor, particularly the Quartodecimans, continued to observe the Passover meal (Pascha) on the 14th of Nisan, regardless of the day of the week. Others, especially in Rome, began to shift toward celebrating the resurrection on the following Sunday, eventually developing into what is now Holy Week. The Council of Nicaea (325 AD/CE) later standardized Easter to fall on the Sunday after the first full moon on or after March 21.  Holy Thursday (or Maundy Thursday) echoes these early traditions. Some churches incorporate foot washing ceremonies and Seder-like meals to recall the Last Supper's Jewish context. In 2025, Holy Thursday falls on April 17; in 2026, it falls on April 2.
