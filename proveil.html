Irenaeus, Against Heresies, Book 1, 8:2, cited in The Ante-Nicene Fathers, A. Cleveland Cox, ed. (1885), Vol. 1, p. 327.

Tertullian (c. 155–220) emphasized the importance of substantial coverings for modesty and proper decorum in worship. On the Veiling of Virgins, Chapter 9 (c. 200 AD):

"Is the reason she is allowed to go without a veil so that she can stand out and be noticed as she enters the church? Is it so she can show off her holiness by leaving her head uncovered? If she deserves recognition, wouldn’t it be better to honor her with some kind of leadership role or responsibility, rather than letting her disregard the practice of covering her head?"

Hippolytus of Rome (c. 170–235), a prominent bishop and theologian, documented early Christian practices in his work Apostolic Tradition, Chapter 18 (verse 5) (c. 215). He wrote:

"All women should cover their heads with a proper veil, not just a piece of thin linen, as that doesn’t count as a true covering."


These writers came from diverse backgrounds, ranging from the Greek-speaking East to the Latin West, and included theologians, bishops, and monastic leaders. Despite their differences, they all agreed that head coverings were a way to show modesty, humility, and respect for apostolic teachings.

Evidence 12

On the whole, modern interpreters largely agreed that the covering Paul required was a veil or material headdress until the mid-twentieth century. Although the view that the covering referred to long hair had been popular among various groups throughout the 20th century, Abel Isaakson was the first to present an extended scholarly argument for this position in print. His work, Marriage and Ministry in the New Temple (1965), offered five key arguments:

  1. Contemporary Judaism had no prohibition against men covering their heads during worship; in fact, priests were required to wear headgear (e.g., Exodus 28:36–40; Ezekiel 44:18).

  2. The Greek phrase ἀκατακαλύπτῳ τῇ κεφαλῇ corresponds to the Hebrew phrase for "loose hair hanging down," as seen in the Septuagint (e.g., Leviticus 13:45; Numbers 5:18).

  3. The context of the passage focuses on hair, suggesting that the implied object of ἔχων is τὴν κόμην ("long hair").

  4. Verse 14 specifically identifies long hair as degrading for men.

  5. Paul’s use of the term κεφαλή ("head") ties the discussion of hair to his broader theological argument.

More recently, a growing number of scholars have independently reached similar conclusions. In a recent article, Francis Watson notes that this interpretation is gaining "broad consensus in recent scholarship." This view can be further divided into two camps:

  • Those who see Paul’s concern as primarily about hair style for both men and women.

  • Those who focus on hair condition, arguing that men must avoid long hair (as it is feminine) and women must avoid loose hair, instead putting it up neatly.

Despite this shift, the traditional interpretation—that Paul is referring to a material head-covering—continues to have contemporary supporters.


Brown, A. Philip (2011). A Survey of the History of the Interpretation of 1 Corinthians 11:2–16. Aldersgate Forum. pp. 1-17.

Evidence 17

Naselli explains that head coverings in 1 Corinthians 11:2–16 were a deliberate practice reflecting Christian theological principles, distinct from Greco-Roman customs. For example, Roman priests, often men of high social status, pulled their togas over their heads during pagan ceremonies, a practice Paul instructed Christian men to avoid to maintain their distinct identity. Similarly, a woman’s head covering, such as a thin scarf, symbolized modesty, chastity, and submission to her husband, marking her as married in the Greco-Roman context. At the time, some rebellious wives in Roman society rejected this symbol, often indicating sexual promiscuity by removing their veils, further underscoring the countercultural nature of Paul’s teaching. Paul also emphasized the universal principle behind these practices: men and women should visibly reflect God’s design for gender distinctions and marital roles in culturally appropriate ways. Additionally, head coverings served as a testimony to angels, highlighting the spiritual significance of maintaining order and submission during worship. Despite emphasizing distinct roles, Paul noted the interdependence of men and women (1 Cor. 11:11–12), affirming that God’s design for gender relationships is both good and complementary.

  1. Custom and Conformity (1 Corinthians 11:16):
    "If anyone in his audience is inclined (cf. 3:18; 8:2; 10:12) to be contentious about the matter of male and female hair styles for those praying or prophesying in their communal worship, 'we,' not just Paul alone (as in 11:2) but also those associated with him, do not have such a custom of allowing either men to pray or prophesy with long (feminine) hair or women to pray or prophesy with short (masculine) hair."
    "Paul adds that neither do the churches of God have such a custom (11:16b). In order for the Corinthians, then, truly to be a church of God that does everything for the glory of God (10:31b) they too must behave, while praying or prophesying in communal worship, in accord with the scriptural order of God's creation (11:3-15), as do all of the other churches."
  1. Interrelationship and Interdependence of Man and Woman (1 Corinthians 11:2-16):
    "The main theme of 1 Cor 11:2-16 is the interrelationship and interdependence between man and woman in a liturgical setting of praying or prophesying. First Corinthians 11:2-16 illustrates how man and woman, when they pray or prophesy together and interdependently, can give glory to God."
  1. Man and Woman as "Glory" (1 Corinthians 11:7):
    "Man is the 'glory of God' (11:7a) in the sense that he radiates and manifests the glory that God bestowed on him within the scriptural created order. Similarly, that the woman is the 'glory of man' (11:7b) means both that the woman radiates and manifests the glory she received by being created from the side of man (Gen 2:21-23) and that the woman, as the suitable 'helper' of man (Gen 2:18, 20), renders honor or gives glory to the man. As the 'glory of man,' then, the woman completes the creation of mankind in the image of God in accord with Gen 1:27: 'And God made the man, according to the image of God he made him, male and female he made them.'
  1. Maintaining Creational Differentiation (1 Corinthians 11:10):
    "Because she is the glory of man in the order of creation, the woman should have 'authority' or 'control' over her literal head (11:10a), meaning that she should wear her hair long while praying or prophesying in communal worship with men. By doing so, she will maintain and respect the differentiation between males and females established by the scriptural order of creation and thus be acting in accord with her creational status as the glory of man.

      • Mark Goodacre critiques Martin's interpretation, arguing that περιβόλαιον does not lexically mean “testicle.”
      • Goodacre examines the two texts Martin cites (Euripides’ Hercules Furens and Achilles Tatius’ Leucippe and Clitophon) and finds them insufficient to support the translation.

      • In Hercules Furens, the term περιβόλαιον refers to a metaphorical “vesture” or “clothing,” not a body part.

      • In Leucippe and Clitophon, περιβόλαιον describes the “overlapping” or “embracing” of leaves, not a direct reference to anatomy.

Drawing on this tradition, an assumption has developed in scholarship that veiling became a social requirement in the fourth century when Christianity became the official religion and the ideas of churchmen gained increasing influence.

    Veiling, however, was not an innovation of early Christianity. For centuries women across the Mediterranean had worn veils of all kinds, and much scholarship has examined these practices, particularly in the Greek world but also in the ancient Near East and the Roman empire. 5 While these practices differed signifi- cantly across time and place, veils were generally used as items of female dress that expressed modesty and piety. What makes Late Antiquity stand out is that there are so many surviving texts that speak explicitly about the importance of veiling, describe how it ought to be practised, and criticize women who were perceived not to be veiling properly (or at all). These texts are also unequivocal about the theological significance of veiling: veiling expressed modesty and piety, but also signified feminine submission and inferiority to men in the eyes of God.

    Within modern scholarship, veiling is therefore understandably often perceived as a symbol of the oppression of the Church, a sign that the relative religious liberality of the Roman world was giv- ing way to the dominance of Christianity over medieval society. 6 Were veiling practices really as simple as the Church Fathers make them seem? In a more nuanced analysis of women’s dress, Mary Harlow concluded that covering the head in Late Antiquity was ‘haphazard’ in visual culture and that there appears to have been no clear rule about whether women should cover.7

    This presents us with two contrasting pictures of veiling: strict religious codes of dress on one side and the almost random use of veils on the other. How do we reconcile these ideas? Does either accurately reflect women’s experiences of veiling or wider social expectations? This article argues that neither provides a satisfactory understanding of the complexity and controversy of veiling practices.

Veils were deeply intersectional and situational garments that were used variously to express differences in age, status and occupation, and to mark different occasions and life-stages. Their use also varied across different communities and across time. Finally, but no less importantly, they were also fashion items: their development into elite acces- sories around the turn of the fifth century can be traced partic- ularly clearly.
