FASTING

Introduction

Fasting was central to early Christian life as a discipline of repentance, spiritual preparation, and humility. This guide outlines its biblical and historical foundations, drawing on pre-325 AD sources and the writings of the Church Fathers.

Biblical Principles

Fasting for Spiritual Strength and Preparation
  • Jesus fasted for forty days and nights in the wilderness before beginning His ministry and facing the devil's temptations. Fasting can strengthen you for spiritual challenges and prepare you to stand firm against temptation (Matthew 4:2, Luke 4:2).
  • Jesus taught that prayer and fasting are sometimes required for overcoming significant spiritual obstacles. Fasting enhances your spiritual authority and connection with God during challenges (Matthew 17:21, Mark 9:29)

Fasting as an Act of Longing for Christ and God

  • Jesus explained that His disciples would fast after His departure, signifying fasting as an act of longing and spiritual connection. Fasting expresses your deep desire for Christ’s presence and guidance in your life (Matthew 9:14-15, Mark 2:18-20, Luke 5:33-35).
  • The prophetess Anna fasted as part of her worship and devotion to God. Fasting can be a form of worship, dedicating yourself wholly to God in prayer and praise (Luke 2:37)

Fasting for Guidance and Decision-Making

  • The 1st Century churches fasted to seek God’s direction when commissioning leaders and appointing elders. Fasting helps you seek clarity and wisdom when faced with important decisions or responsibilities (Acts 13:2-3; Acts 14:23).

Fasting with the Right Heart

  • Jesus instructed His followers not to make a show of their fasting, but to do it humbly and privately. Fasting should be done for God, not for the approval or admiration of others (Matthew 6:16-18)
  • Avoiding being Self-Righteous for the wrong reasons. The Pharisee’s boastful fasting serves as a warning against pride in spiritual disciplines (Luke 18:12).

Fasting as a Test of Perseverance

  • The Apostle Paul fasted as part of his dedication to the gospel, enduring both voluntary and involuntary deprivation. Fasting teaches endurance and reliance on God through physical and spiritual challenges (2 Cor 6:5; 2 Cor 11:27).

Practical Applications

Guidelines

  • Sincerity: Fasting should be done with genuine intent, not for outward show.
  • Privacy: Keep your fasting between you and God, avoiding public display
  • Humility: Approach fasting with a humble heart, seeking God's will (Psalm 35:13).

Types of Fasts

  • Complete: Abstaining from all food and drink.
    • Jesus in the Wilderness (Matt 4:1-2)
    • Moses on Mount Sinai (Exodus 34:28)
    • Esther’s Fast (Esther 4:16)
    • Paul’s Conversion (Acts 9:9)
    • Jonah’s Warning to Nineveh (Jonah 3:7)
    • David’s Mourning for Abner (2 Samuel 3:35)
  • Partial: Abstaining from certain foods or meals.
    • Daniel's Diet (Daniel 1:8-16)
    • John the Baptist’s Lifestyle (Matthew 3:4, Mark 1:6)
    • The Nazirite Vow (Numbers 6:1-4)

What occasion?

  • For Repentance and Mourning
    • National (Jonah 3:5-10, 1 Samuel 7:6).
    • Personal (Psalm 35:13, Daniel 9:3-5).
    • Mourning (2 Samuel 1:12, Nehemiah 1:4).
  • For Seeking Guidance
    • For God's will
    • For spiritual battles
  • For Seeking God’s Protection from Danger
    • Esther’s fast (Esther 4:16).
    • Ezra’s fast (Ezra 8:21-23).
  • For Spiritual Renewal and Dedication
    • Jesus’ fast (Matthew 4:1-2).
    • Paul’s fast (Acts 9:9).
  • As a Regular Discipline
    • Anna’s fasting (Luke 2:37).
    • Pharisee’s fasting (Luke 18:12).
  • In Times of Crisis
    • Jehoshaphat’s fast (2 Chronicles 20:3-4).
    • Joel’s call to fast (Joel 1:14, 2:12).

When to fast?

  • Regularly (Ch. 8, Verse 1 of the Didache)
    • Wednesday, Betrayal of Jesus by Judas
    • Friday, Crucifixion of Jesus
    • Lasted from sunrise to sunset, breaking fast in the evening (Time depends on timezone)
  • One Day
    • David’s fast for Saul and Jonathan (2 Samuel 1:12).
    • The Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16:29-31).
    • This fast was observed until evening.
  • Three Days
    • Esther’s fast (Esther 4:16).
    • Paul’s fast (Acts 9:9).
    • The fast likely concluded at the end of the third day, possibly in the evening.
  • Seven Days
    • Mourning for Saul (1 Samuel 31:13).
    • The fast likely ended on the evening of the seventh day.
  • Twenty-One-Days
    • Daniel’s fast (Daniel 10:2-3).
    • Conducted with light nourishment or fluids.
  • Forty-Days
    • Moses’ fasts (Exodus 34:28, Deuteronomy 9:9, 18).
    • Elijah’s fast (1 Kings 19:8).
    • Jesus’ fast (Matthew 4:1-2).
    • Sustained supernaturally by God.

Other circumstances (The Didache)

  • Fast for those who persecute you (Ch. 8, Verse 1)
  • Fast before baptism, for baptizer, candidate, and wider community (Ch 7, Verse 4)
    • Fast for one or two days beforehand
    • Time of self-denial, prayer, and reflection

Diet

Complete

No food or liquids

Partial

Daniel's Diet: Daniel and his companions abstained from the king’s rich foods and wine, consuming only vegetables (grains, legumes, and fruits) and water for ten days initially. Later, Daniel observed a similar fast for 21 days, avoiding “pleasant bread,” meat, and wine.

John the Baptist: John's diet of locusts and wild honey reflects his ascetic lifestyle and dependence on natural provisions. Locusts, considered clean according to Levitical law (Leviticus 11:22), were a common food source in the region, especially for those living in the wilderness. Wild honey provided a natural sweetener and energy source.

The Nazirite Vow: Individuals taking the Nazirite vow abstained from wine, fermented drinks, and anything derived from grapes, as outlined in Numbers 6:1-4. This partial abstention was a form of dedication and consecration to God.

Breaking fast

  • After breaking the fast, there is no specific prohibitions on what could be consumed.
    • Believers are free from the strict requirements of the Mosaic Law, including dietary restrictions (Romans 14:14, 1 Corinthians 8:8). "For the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking, but of righteousness, peace, and joy in the Holy Spirit." (Romans 14:17).

The Steps

  1. Set Your Intention (Refer to What occasion?)
  2. Prepare Spiritually
    • Prayer
    • Repentance
    • Define the Purpose
  3. Choose the Type of Fast (Refer to Types of Fasts)
  4. Wash Your Face and Anoint Your Head with Oil (Preferably Olive Oil)
    • Matthew 6:17
  5. Focus on Prayer and Scripture
    • Use the time you would normally spend eating to pray, read the Bible, and seek God.
