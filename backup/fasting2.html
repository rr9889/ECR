FASTING

Fasting was central to early Christian life as a discipline of repentance, spiritual preparation, and humility.

Biblical Purposes

Spiritual Strength and Preparation

  • Jesus fasted for 40 days in the wilderness, overcoming the devil’s temptations and preparing for His ministry (Matthew 4:1-11).
  • Jesus taught that some spiritual challenges, like casting out demons, require prayer and fasting (Matthew 17:21).

Act of Longing for God

  • Jesus said His disciples would fast after His departure, showing fasting as an act of longing for His presence (Matthew 9:14-15).
  • The prophetess Anna fasted and prayed continually, worshiping God and waiting for the Messiah (Luke 2:36-37).

Guidance and Decision-Making

  • The church in Antioch fasted and prayed before sending Barnabas and Saul for missionary work, seeking God’s guidance (Acts 13:2-3).
  • Paul and Barnabas fasted and prayed when appointing elders, showing fasting’s role in seeking wisdom for leadership (Acts 14:23).

As a Test of Perseverance

  • Paul fasted as part of his dedication to the gospel, enduring both voluntary and involuntary deprivation, teaching endurance and reliance on God (2 Corinthians 6:5, 11:27).

For Deliverance and Protection

  • Esther and the Jews fasted for three days, and God delivered them from Haman’s plot (Esther 4:16, 7:10).
  • Ezra and the exiles fasted, and God protected them on their journey to Jerusalem (Ezra 8:21-23).

For Repentance and Renewal

  • The people of Nineveh fasted and repented, and God spared the city from destruction (Jonah 3:5-10).
  • David fasted and prayed after his sin with Bathsheba, and God forgave him (Psalm 51:1-17).

For Spiritual Breakthrough

  • Daniel fasted and prayed for 21 days, and an angel brought him understanding of future events (Daniel 10:2-3, 12-13).
  • Jehoshaphat and Judah fasted, and God defeated their enemies without a fight (2 Chronicles 20:3-4, 22-24).

Occasions for Daily Life

  • Seeking Guidance: Fast when facing important decisions, such as career changes, relationships, or major life choices (Acts 13:2-3).
  • Overcoming Challenges: Fast for strength and clarity during difficult times, such as financial struggles, health issues, or personal conflicts (Matthew 17:21).
  • Repentance: Fast when seeking forgiveness for mistakes or sins, as a way to humble yourself and draw closer to God (Jonah 3:5-10).
  • Forgiveness: Fast when asking for God’s mercy and healing after personal failures or broken relationships (Psalm 51:1-17).
  • Worship and Devotion: Fast as a way to deepen your relationship with God, especially during seasons of spiritual reflection or renewal (Luke 2:37).
  • Protection: Fast when seeking God’s protection for yourself, your family, or loved ones during times of danger or uncertainty (Ezra 8:21-23).
  • Revelation: Fast when seeking clarity, wisdom, or insight into a specific situation or problem (Daniel 10:2-3).
  • Victory: Fast when facing a significant challenge or obstacle, asking God for victory and breakthrough (2 Chronicles 20:3-4).
  • Preparation for a New Season: Fast when starting a new chapter in life, such as a new job, marriage, or ministry, to seek God’s blessing and direction (Matthew 4:2).

Practical Applications

Guidelines

  • Sincerity: Fasting should be done with genuine intent, not for outward show.
  • Privacy: Keep your fasting between you and God, avoiding public display.
  • Humility: Approach fasting with a humble heart, seeking God's will (Psalm 35:13).

Types of Fasts

  • Complete: Abstaining from all food and drink.
    • Jesus in the Wilderness (Matt 4:1-2)
    • Moses on Mount Sinai (Exodus 34:28)
    • Esther’s Fast (Esther 4:16)
    • Paul’s Conversion (Acts 9:9)
    • Jonah’s Warning to Nineveh (Jonah 3:7)
    • David’s Mourning for Abner (2 Samuel 3:35)
  • Partial: Abstaining from certain foods or meals.
    • Daniel's Diet (Daniel 1:8-16)
    • John the Baptist’s Lifestyle (Matthew 3:4, Mark 1:6)
    • The Nazirite Vow (Numbers 6:1-4)

When to Fast?

Regularly

  • Wednesday: Betrayal of Jesus by Judas.
  • Friday: Crucifixion of Jesus.
  • Lasted from sunrise to sunset, breaking fast in the evening (Time depends on timezone).

One Day

  • David’s fast for Saul and Jonathan (2 Samuel 1:12).
  • The Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16:29-31).
  • This fast was observed until evening.

Three Days

  • Esther’s fast (Esther 4:16).
  • Paul’s fast (Acts 9:9).
  • The fast likely concluded at the end of the third day, possibly in the evening.

Seven Days

  • Mourning for Saul (1 Samuel 31:13).
  • The fast likely ended on the evening of the seventh day.

Twenty-One Days

  • Daniel’s fast (Daniel 10:2-3).
  • Conducted with light nourishment or fluids.

Forty Days

  • Moses’ fasts (Exodus 34:28, Deuteronomy 9:9, 18).
  • Elijah’s fast (1 Kings 19:8).
  • Jesus’ fast (Matthew 4:1-2).
  • Sustained supernaturally by God.

Diet

Complete Fast

No food or liquids.

Partial Fast

  • Daniel's Diet: Daniel and his companions abstained from the king’s rich foods and wine, consuming only vegetables (grains, legumes, and fruits) and water for ten days initially. Later, Daniel observed a similar fast for 21 days, avoiding “pleasant bread,” meat, and wine.
  • John the Baptist: John's diet of locusts and wild honey reflects his ascetic lifestyle and dependence on natural provisions. Locusts, considered clean according to Levitical law (Leviticus 11:22), were a common food source in the region, especially for those living in the wilderness. Wild honey provided a natural sweetener and energy source.
  • The Nazirite Vow: Individuals taking the Nazirite vow abstained from wine, fermented drinks, and anything derived from grapes, as outlined in Numbers 6:1-4. This partial abstention was a form of dedication and consecration to God.

Breaking Fast

  • After breaking the fast, there is no specific prohibition on what could be consumed.
    • Believers are free from the strict requirements of the Mosaic Law, including dietary restrictions (Romans 14:14, 1 Corinthians 8:8). "For the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking, but of righteousness, peace, and joy in the Holy Spirit." (Romans 14:17).

The Steps

  1. Set Your Intention:
    • Determine the reason for your fast (e.g., guidance, repentance, spiritual growth).
  2. Prepare Spiritually:
    • Prayer: Begin with prayer, asking God for guidance.
    • Repentance: Confess sins and seek forgiveness (1 John 1:9).
    • Define the Purpose: Clearly articulate your spiritual goals.
  3. Choose the Type of Fast:
    • Complete Fast: No food or drink (Esther 4:16).
    • Partial Fast: Abstain from specific foods or meals (Daniel 1:8-16).
  4. Wash and Anoint:
    • Wash Your Face: Maintain normal hygiene to avoid drawing attention (Matthew 6:17).
    • Anoint with [Olive] Oil: Symbolizes joy and dedication (Psalm 23:5).
  5. Focus on Prayer and Scripture:
    • Use meal times for prayer, Bible study, or worship.
    • Scripture Example: Acts 13:2-3 – “While worshiping and fasting, the Holy Spirit spoke.”
  6. Break Your Fast Wisely:
    • Breaking can be done after sunset depending on the goals.
    • Start Light: Reintroduce food gradually (e.g., fruits, soups).
    • Thank God: Reflect on blessings and spiritual growth (Romans 14:17).
